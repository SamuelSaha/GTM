Introduction
It seems as if it's all anyone can talk about these days: entrepreneurship, industry disruptors, and
the almighty startup
. As technology grows and the world gets smaller, barriers to access weaken, and normal people have opportunities to innovate and create industries that didn’t exist a decade ago.
While rags-to-riches stories seemingly happen overnight, the fact is that the most successful entrepreneurs dedicate time to learn about entrepreneurship and the startup process. They have immersed themselves in the culture, language, and nuance of Silicon Valley. There is much to absorb if you are just entering the world of startups for both aspiring founders and investors. There is a different language you must learn.
As someone on the inside, working in a leadership role at a major tech firm and serving as an angel investor, I can attest that there are two languages spoken in Silicon Valley.
In one line of speech, you hear familiar business jargon like "profit" and "growth." That is a basic conversation that almost anyone can follow, one that varies little whether you are talking about the new local bakery or a sneaker factory. The other language? That is the one we're interested in, and it's a touch harder to understand. In this language, you hear terms like
unicorn, allocation and SaaS.
This is the language that you’ll need to familiarize yourself with to understand or participate in insider conversations, in order to navigate the world of startups, and apply this knowledge to your own business dealings.
Before I spell out the
The Ultimate Startup Dictionary for Beginners
definitions, let me tell you a little bit about myself.
A graduate of New York University's Stern School of Business, I majored in Finance. While living in New York City, in 2013 I began angel investing, but in order to improve access to quality deals, I knew that I needed to relocate to the Bay Area and immerse myself in the epicenter of startup culture, Silicon Valley. Since moving to San Francisco I have served as a Vice President at a large financial services firm, worked in leadership positions at two major tech startup companies, and have invested in over 100 early-stage startups including 6 unicorns (companies valued at over $1 billion).
In meeting many startup founders, what I have found time and again, is that as much passion and imagination as they may have, there is sometimes a lack of knowledge about the process. More often than not, when I hear someone talking about their startup idea, my first thought is not that the idea is unfeasible – but that the founders have not done the groundwork needed to prepare themselves for the essential first steps such as fundraising, let alone a launch or expansion. Many times they are unfamiliar with the processes, culture, and language of Silicon Valley and would benefit from an insider’s perspective.
Founders get fired up when they feel they have come up with a grand plan or because they have assembled an impressive team. There is nothing wrong with that and no one should be discouraged from living their dreams. But aspiring entrepreneurs quickly learn that there is more to starting a business than having enthusiasm. There is more to developing a successful startup than creating an innovative product or service, or hiring the best coders. One of the best things you can
do as an aspiring founder or investor is to prepare yourself to gain entry into the inner circles of startup culture.
Once you start perusing the pages of this book, you may find that some of the terms intrigue you, or as though you’re learning a foreign language. Or perhaps, you’ll find that you don’t have to read it from cover-to-cover, but rather use it as a reference guide to go back to when necessary. Honor whatever method of learning works best for you. Whether you are in the concept phase, have conducted a friends and family funding round, or if you have just gotten done binge-watching
Shark Tank
, ask yourself where you would need to know each of these terms.
Familiarizing yourself with these terms will put you miles ahead of much of your competition. You can then proceed more boldly and ambitiously towards your goals and dreams..
Happy learning – and happy
founding
!

Angel Investor Types
accredited Investor
An accredited investor is a wealthy individual potentially interested in investing in a company. According to the Securities and Exchange Commission: “A natural person with income exceeding $200,000 in each of the two most recent years or joint income with spouse exceeding $300,000 for those years and a reasonable expectation of the same income level in the current year; or A natural person who has individual net worth, or joint net worth with the person’s spouse, that exceeds $1 million at the time of the purchase, excluding the value of the primary residence of such person.”
angel investor
An angel investor is someone, usually a high-net-worth individual, who offers financial backing to startups in exchange for partial ownership of the company.
angel group
An angel group is a group of angel investors who collectively invest in early stage startups. Angel groups are sometimes formed around affinity groups (e.g. XYZ University Alumni), sector (e.g. healthcare), region (e.g. north east), etc.
super angel
A super angel is a venture capitalist who has served as an angel investor but since gone on to launch a venture capital fund. They may operate as a hybrid of the two, retaining some
characteristics of angel investing despite their new level of influence.
One example of a super angel is Ron Conway, who has invested in over 500 startups throughout his career. Conway is known for his early investments in companies such as Google, PayPal, and Facebook, which have all become incredibly successful.
Another example of a super angel is Naval Ravikant, who has invested in companies such as Twitter, Uber, and Yammer.

Board Types
advisory board
An advisory board is separate and distinct from the Board of Directors. The advisory board members provide advice ranging from fundraising, domain expertise, strategy and key introductions.
board of directors
A board of directors is a group of people who cast votes to make decisions about the executive leadership at a company. The board wields the final say over the most important hiring and firing decisions.

Business Types
B2B
B2B stands for “business to business” and refers to a transaction that happens directly between one business and another.
An example of a B2B company is IBM, which provides technology solutions and consulting services to businesses.
Another example of a B2B company is Salesforce, which offers a cloud-based platform for customer relationship management (CRM) to businesses.
B2C
B2C stands for “business to consumer” and refers to the process of selling services or products directly from a business to a consumer, or end-user.
An example of a B2C company is Amazon, which is the world's largest online retailer, offering a wide range of products to consumers.
Another example of a B2C company is Nike, which designs and sells athletic shoes, apparel, and accessories to consumers around the world.
cottage business
A cottage business, or cottage industry, is a small-scale, decentralized manufacturing business often operating out of someone's home.
An example of a cottage business is a small bakery that operates from the owner's home kitchen. The owner may bake cakes and
pastries to sell to local customers or supply them to nearby coffee shops and restaurants.
Another example of a cottage business is a home-based daycare service.
lifestyle business
A lifestyle business is a company established and operated by its founders for the purpose of developing and maintaining a particular lifestyle or level of income.
An example of a lifestyle business is a boutique hotel that is owned and operated by an individual or family, who are passionate about hospitality and providing a unique experience for guests.
Another example of a lifestyle business is a small organic farm that is owned and operated by an individual or family, who are passionate about sustainable farming practices and providing fresh, locally grown produce to their community.
A third example of a lifestyle business is a yoga studio that is owned and operated by a yoga instructor, who is passionate about health and wellness and wants to share the benefits of yoga with others.

Customer Terminology
activation
An activation is the point which leads those who have become customers to then become paying customers. They sign up for a premium service or buy a product.
average order value (AOV)
Average order value (AOV) tracks the average dollar amount a customer spends each time they place an order on a website or app.
lead
A lead is someone in your target audience who may be converted into a customer.
letter of intent (LOI)
A letter of intent (LOI) is a common agreement between businesses and potential customers to define commitment, interest, terms, and pricing in writing prior to delivering the goods or services.
retention (customer retention)
Retention is the ability of a product or service to hold a customer’s attention and foster loyalty. When retention is high, growth tends to continue unabated.
target audience
A target audience is a group of people for whom you are developing your product or service. These people have either
expressed or exhibited a need for a particular product or service.

Customer Types
business customers
These are customers who purchase products or services for their businesses. Business customers may make purchasing decisions based on factors such as cost-effectiveness, reliability, and compatibility with existing systems or processes.
early adopter
An early adopter is someone who buys or uses a company’s product or service before anyone else does.
An example of an early adopter is someone who purchased the first generation iPhone when it was released in 2007, even though it was a relatively new and untested product at the time.
evangelist
An evangelist is a customer who recruits other people to use a product or service.They exhibit a true passion for a startup, without any ulterior motives.
government customers
These are customers who purchase products or services for government agencies. Government customers may make purchasing decisions based on factors such as compliance with regulations, cost-effectiveness, and environmental impact.
individual consumers
These are customers who purchase products or services for their personal use. Individual consumers may make purchasing
decisions based on factors such as price, quality, convenience, or brand loyalty.
institutional customers
These are customers who purchase products or services for organizations such as schools, hospitals, or non-profit organizations. Institutional customers may make purchasing decisions based on factors such as cost-effectiveness, quality, and compliance with regulations.
International customers
These are customers located in other countries who purchase products or services. International customers may make purchasing decisions based on factors such as cultural differences, language barriers, and international regulations.

Debt & Security Instruments Terminology
capped note
A capped note refers to a cap placed on investor notes in a round of financing. Founders and investors agree to place a cap on the valuation of the company where notes turn to equity, which means investors will own a certain percentage of a company relative to that cap when another round of funding is raised. Uncapped rounds are generally more favorable to a startup.
capped participation
Capped participation, also known as “capped participating preferred,” is the middle ground between participating preferred stock and non-participating preferred stock. The cap is typically fixed as a multiple of the original investment.
common stock
Common stock is a type of stock issued to the majority of shareholders in a company who enjoy certain rights that their counterparts in preferred stockholders do not. Common stockholders derive value from their shares when the company grows, rather than receiving regular payouts.
convertible note/convertible stock
A convertible note or convertible stock is an agreement that grants the holder the right to purchase a set number of shares in a company.
equity
Equity represents the amount of capital invested or owned by the owner of a company. It is evaluated by the difference between liabilities and assets recorded on a company’s balance sheet.
simple agreement for future equity (SAFE)
A simple agreement for future equity (SAFE) is a legal agreement that guarantees ownership shares for distribution at a later date.
non-participating preferred stock
Non-participating preferred specifically limits the amount of dividends paid to its holders; offering specifically-mandated dividend percentages stated on the face of the stock certificate.
uncapped note
Uncapped note, relates to the valuation setting the maximum price on a convertible security being “uncapped.” It allows investors to convert the outstanding balance into shares of stock at either the valuation cap or the price per share in qualified financing.
warrant
A warrant is a derivative security that allows the holder to buy equity at a certain price during a particular window of time.

Design Terminology
accessibility
Accessibility and usability relate to functionality of computer applications and web design. Combining these standards and processes enable people of different abilities to access and utilize applications.
design thinking
Design thinking is a creative problem solving method for identifying and addressing issues that a startup can solve for customers. It is an abstract and intensive departure from the linear thinking of the corporate world.
responsive design
Responsive design is a layout for a website or web app that displays differently from one device to the next and from one screen resolution to the next.
user experience (UX)
The user experience (UX) is the customer-facing aspect of a product, application, system, or service and that defines how they perceive or experience it.
user interface (UI)
The user interface (UI) is the platform enabling customers to access a product or service.
wireframe
A wireframe is a visual representation of the key elements of
a user interface, the intended layout, and demonstrates the functionality of a particular system.

Employee & Employee Equity Terminology
cliff
A cliff is the time an employee needs to work at a startup before any of their promised equity vests – that is, becomes available to them.
employee option pool
An employee option pool consists of shares of stock reserved for employees of a private company. It is a method of attracting talented employees to a startup; those who get in early will usually receive a greater percentage of the option pool than those who arrive later.
exercise price (also strike price)
The exercise price, also known as the strike price, is the price of an underlying security that can be bought or sold when trading.
lock-up
A lock-up is the period during which founders, investors, and employees cannot sell their stocks following a public offering. Normally, a lock-up runs from six months to one year.
separation agreement
A separation agreement is a contractual understanding between an employer and a soon-to-be terminated employee.
stock option
A stock option is the right, but not the obligation, to purchase or
sell a share of stock at a specific price within a specified period of time. There are two types of options: puts, which is a bet that a stock will fall, or calls, which is a bet that a stock will rise.
vesting
Vesting is the point at which a company, its employees, and investors fully own the shares that their options have granted them. It may not define the time at which the shares can be sold.

Engineering Terminology
application programming interface (API)
Application programming interface (API) is a go-between for two programs. It enables one program to access information or features on another program in a way that is safe and convenient.
back-end
The back-end is the technology that supports the front-end. It includes the servers used to host and the databases used to store information.
A/B testing
A/B testing is a test method for determining which of two things (products, pitches, advertisements, etc.) people prefer. Testers randomize two things people in a test group experience and then record their reactions.
agile
Agile development is when a team emphasizes flexibility. Instead of "one person, one role," it will find multi-talented, multi-skilled leaders who can take ownership over larger pieces of projects to build incrementally and iteratively while testing quickly.
application programming interface (API)
Application programming interface (API) is a go-between for two programs. It enables one program to access information
or features on another program in a way that is safe and convenient.
back-end
The back-end is the technology that supports the front-end. It includes the servers used to host and the databases used to store information.
compiler
A compiler is a computer program that translates computer source code written in one high-level programming language into another language.
concurrency
Concurrency is an aspect of some software programs: features can run one-by-one, rather than all at once, and still yield the same results that they would have as part of the program.
create, read, update and delete (CRUD)
CRUD is a term describing the four functions necessary to maintain a database: create, read, update, and delete.
database
A database is an organized collection of data stored and accessed electronically. Small databases may be stored on a computer file system, while large databases are hosted on computer clusters or in cloud storage.
DevOps
DevOps is a combination of software developers (dev) and operations (ops) employed to increase the efficiency, speed, and
security of software development and delivery.
front-end
The front-end is all of the technology that the end user sees; including websites, apps, and application programming interface (API).
full-stack
A full stack is a descriptor for a developer who is capable of overseeing multiple aspects of a project. Normally, they will know several programming languages: HTML, CSS, PHP, MySQL, Python, and Flask, for example.
open-source
Open-source is a descriptor for any software or hardware in which the functions and mechanisms are available to the public. Many developers prefer open-source models because it encourages widespread collaboration and stronger security.
stack
A stack is a set of professional skills or tools that teams bring to a project in order to achieve a desired result.

Exit Terminology
acqui-hire
An acqui-hire is a buyout that emphasizes the talent and skill assembled at a startup. It happens when another company wants to acquire the people more than anything else at a company.
acquisition
An acquisition is a buyout in which one company "folds into" another company. All of the technology, employees, and intellectual property merges into one.
buyout
A buyout is a process by which a company or an individual purchases enough shares in a startup to control the votes as defined in the articles of incorporation.
exit
An exit is a process by which founders and investors leave a startup, normally because of a buyout by a larger company. It connotes a successful outcome for everyone involved.
exit strategy (also liquidity event)
An exit strategy,also known as a liquidity event, is the way in which a venture capitalist or business owner gets out of an investment. It is the fund’s intended method for liquidating its holdings while achieving the maximum possible return and depends upon the exit climates, including market conditions and industry trends.
exit velocity
Exit velocity is originally a baseball term, referring to the speed of a ball as it is immediately hit off a bat. In business, it refers to the speed at which an exit or liquidity event takes place after an investment.
home run
A home run refers to a company that has an exit which returns twenty or more times investors' initial capital.
initial public offering (IPO)
Initial public offering (IPO) is the fundraising stage during which startups make ownership available on a stock exchange. It is normally the largest fundraising stage.
late-stage
Late-stage is any of the possible conclusions to a startup: an exit, an initial offering, or a bankruptcy. It is neither positive nor negative, only defining the distance from the initial launch to the later stage event.
leveraged buyout (LBO)
A leveraged buyout occurs when a company is acquired with mostly debt and little equity. The buyer uses assets from the purchased company as collateral and plans to pay off the debt using future cash flow.
liquidation
Liquidation is an event that could result in either investors or debt holders receiving cash from a company through acquisition or a sale of assets resulting from a bankruptcy.
merger
A merger is the process by which two companies become one. It often connotes a situation in which neither company is exerting dominance over the other company.
private IPO
A private IPO is the process of raising high volumes of capital through private placements. Not technically a “public offering,” these placements are offered only to accredited investors such as pension funds, investment banks and certain mutual funds that meet the criteria laid down by the company offering a private IPO.
special purpose acquisition company (SPAC)
A special purpose acquisition company (SPAC) is formed to raise money through an initial public offering (IPO) for the purpose of acquiring another company.

Financial Terminology
balance sheet
A balance sheet is a summary of all the accounting at a company. It lists the "money in" and the "money out," as well as a final calculation of assets and cash available.
break even point (BEP)
Breakeven, or breakeven point, is the point at which cost and income are equal and there is neither profit nor loss; having income exactly equal to expenditure.
bubble
A bubble is an economic cycle characterized by a fast market value rise, particularly in the price of assets. This fast inflation may be followed by a quick decrease in value that is referred to as a "crash" or a "bubble burst."
capital
Capital is the money a business has available to pay for its daily operations as well as to fund its future growth. The four major types of capital include working capital, debt, equity, and trading capital.
capital expenditure
Capital expenditure is the money that is used to purchase assets through a company. It entails investments outside of day-to-day upkeep spending.
capital call
A capital call also known as a "draw down," is the act of collecting funds from limited partners. It is a legal right of an investment firm to demand a portion of the capital promised to it by an investor.
capital efficiency
Capital efficiency is the relationship between expenses incurred by the company and how much money is used to manufacture goods or services.
cap table
A cap table, or capitalization table, is a breakdown of a company's ownership percentages, equity dilution, and equity value in each round of investment by founders, investors, and other owners.
cash flow
Cash flow is the liquid money that moves in and out of a company. If the cash flow is positive, then the company is profitable, and if it is negative, then it is operating at a loss.
cash flow statement
A cash flow statement is a spreadsheet outlining current "money in" and "money out." It is similar to a balance sheet but also includes projected transactions.
cash position
A cash position represents the amount of cash that a company, investor, fund, or bank has on its accounting books at a given time.
carried interest
Carried interest, or “carry,” is the share of profits an investment manager is entitled to keep as compensation. It may also be referred to as an “incentive fee” or a “performance fee.”
closing
Closing relates to the closing or ending of a sales transaction. It occurs when the seller and buyer agree to the conditions of a contract and the buyer makes a firm commitment.
cost of council
Cost of council refers to any operating or program costs incurred by the council or the fiscal agent in connection with the activities and operations of the council or its programs.
dilution
Dilution is the process by which founders and investors lose their true equity in a company, by offering their shares in exchange for some form of capital.
dividends
A dividend is the distribution of corporate profits to eligible shareholders by publicly listed companies to reward investors for investing in the venture.
earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization (EBITDA)
EBITDA is a reliable measurement of the true stability of a startup. It stands for "earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization."
fair market value (FMV)
Fair market value (FMV) is the price an asset sells for on the open market.
fiduciary
A fiduciary may be a person or organization that acts on behalf of another, with a duty to preserve trust and good faith, being bound legally and ethically.
gross margin
The gross margin is the difference between revenue and cost of goods sold (COGS), divided by revenue, sometimes used to refer to gross profit margin.
leverage
Leverage refers to the capital structure of a company; the amount of debt a startup has in its mix of debt and equity. A company with more debt than average for its industry is referred to as “highly leveraged.”
net profit
Net profit, also known as net income, is the amount of money a business earns after all operating costs, interest, and tax expenses are deducted.
recapitalization
Recapitalization is the process of reorganizing a company's capital structure.
revenue
Revenue is the money generated by a company. It includes
profit, as well as the money needed to keep a company afloat.
return on investment (ROI)
Return on investment (ROI) is the money a product, service, or investment generates, relative to the capital and time that it has taken to build.
success fee
A success fee is a payment made to an investment bank for successfully closing a transaction.

Financing Types
bootstrapped
Being bootstrapped is a descriptor for a startup at which the founders have contributed the seed capital. It usually requires a lean, agile approach to hiring and management.
bridge loan
A bridge loan is a financial loan that a bank or investor provides to cover well-defined expenses to carry a startup from a period of non-profitability to profitability.
business plan competition
A business plan competition is a contest between startups or early-stage businesses with a goal of developing original ideas or completing existing business plans based on specific contest guidelines. Business plan competitions often have a financial award for the winner which can be used as seed capital for your startup.
crowdfunding
Crowdfunding is a type of fundraising for a project or venture whereby money is raised in small amounts from a large number of people, typically through the internet.
debt financing
Debt financing refers to a method of raising funds for a business by borrowing money from lenders, such as banks, bondholders, or other financial institutions. In this type of financing, the
borrower is required to repay the borrowed amount, along with interest, over a fixed period of time.
equity financing
Equity financing is a method of raising capital for a business or organization by selling ownership shares to investors in exchange for their investment. In this type of financing, the investors become part owners of the company and share in its profits and losses.
growth equity
Growth equity is typically a private equity investment, usually a minority investment, in a relatively mature company that is looking for capital to expand or restructure operations, enter new markets, or finance a significant acquisition without a change of control of the business.
party round
A party round is a round of financing in which a small amount of money is raised from a large number of investors, usually between 10 and 20.
syndicate
A syndicate is a group of venture capitalists who participate in an investment round.

Founder Terminology
co-founder
This refers to an individual who starts a company with one or more other individuals. Co-founders may share the responsibilities of running the company and have equal or different levels of ownership.
founder
A founder is a person who starts their own company. The title of founder offers a clear indication about the history and creation of a company. While the titles of CEO or owner may be passed from one person to another, this title cannot be transferred, since the founding of a company is a one-time event.
wantrepreneur
A wantrepreneur is someone who has dreams of starting a business, who thinks and talks about it, but never actually does so.

Funding Rounds & Funding Sources
accelerator
An accelerator is a professional program designed to prepare startup founders to make pitches in front of venture capital firms or public showcase events. Startup accelerators often provide coaching, education and capital in exchange for a percentage of equity.
bootstrapped
Being bootstrapped is a descriptor for a startup at which the founders have contributed the seed capital. It usually requires a lean, agile approach to hiring and management.
bridge loan
A bridge loan is a financial loan that a bank or investor provides to cover well-defined expenses to carry a startup from a period of non-profitability to profitability.
business plan competition
A business plan competition is a contest between startups or early-stage businesses with a goal of developing original ideas or completing existing business plans based on specific contest guidelines. Competition prizes can include a non-dilutive startup grant.
down round
A down round is when a startup does an additional round of funding, but instead of selling each new share for more than the previous per-share price, they go for less.
first-round financing

Startup Laws & Startup Rules
JOBS Act
The Jumpstart Our Business Startups (JOBS) Act, is a piece of US legislation passed in April 2012 which loosens small business regulations. It includes several provisions related to early stage companies, such as lowering disclosure requirements and allowing greater access to crowdfunding.
Regulation A
Reg A, or Regulation A, is a Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) rule that allows private companies to raise capital from the public through a public offering of securities. Reg A is designed to provide smaller companies with an alternative to traditional IPOs or private placements by allowing them to raise up to $50 million in a 12-month period from both accredited and non-accredited investors.
Regulation CF
Regulation CF (Crowdfunding) is a regulation in the United States that allows small businesses and startups to raise capital from the general public through equity crowdfunding platforms. This regulation, which was adopted in 2016 as part of the JOBS Act, is intended to make it easier for small businesses to raise capital and for non-accredited investors to invest in private companies.
Under Regulation CF, companies can raise up to $5 million in a 12-month period from both accredited and non-accredited investors, through crowdfunding platforms registered with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). Companies must
disclose certain information about their business and financials to potential investors, and the amount each investor can invest is limited based on their income and net worth.
Regulation D
Regulation D, or Reg D, is a Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) rule that exempts certain types of private securities offerings from registration requirements under the Securities Act of 1933. Reg D offerings are a popular way for companies to raise capital from accredited investors without undergoing the time and expense of a traditional public offering.
Under Reg D, companies can sell securities to accredited investors without having to register the securities with the SEC. Accredited investors are individuals or institutions that meet certain income or net worth requirements, and are deemed to have the financial sophistication to understand and evaluate the risks of investing in private securities.
Reg D offerings are divided into three separate exemptions, each with different requirements and limitations:
Rule 504: Allows companies to raise up to $5 million in a 12-month period through the sale of securities to accredited and non-accredited investors.
Rule 506(b): Allows companies to raise an unlimited amount of capital from an unlimited number of accredited investors, as well as up to 35 non-accredited investors who have a pre-existing relationship with the company or its principals.
Rule 506(c): Allows companies to raise an unlimited amount of capital from accredited investors only, but requires that the company take steps to verify the accredited status of its investors.

Fully diluted shares are the shares that will be available after no more conversion is possible. They define the final number of shares that make up a company.
grandfather rights
A grandfather rights, also known as grandfather clause, grandfather policy, or grandfathering is a provision in which an old rule continues to apply to some existing situations while a new rule will apply to all future cases. Those exempt from a new rule are said to have grandfather rights or acquired rights, or to have been “grandfathered in.”
information rights
Information rights is a provision, typically found in Investors rights agreements, which requires startups to provide the board of directors updates and financial information to minority shareholders on a periodic basis.
investor’s rights agreement
An investor’s rights agreement is an agreement that is usually required by early or large investors in a startup and may include various provisions.
issued shares
Issued shares are the stock holdings that individuals and companies own in a startup.
liquidity event
A liquidity event is anything that enables founders and investors to sell their ownership shares. It includes public offerings,
acquisitions, and mergers.
liquidation preference
Liquidation preference is a term used in finance and investment to describe a preference given to certain investors or shareholders in the event of a liquidation or sale of a company. In general, liquidation preference refers to the right of a particular class of investors to receive their investment back before other investors or shareholders in the company.
For example, if a company is sold or goes bankrupt, the investors with a liquidation preference would be entitled to receive their investment back before the common shareholders. This can be particularly important in cases where the sale or liquidation of the company results in less money than was initially invested.
no shop clause
No shop is a standard clause in a term sheet which states a founder may not share the terms with other investors in order to “shop” or receive a competing offer.
non-binding
Non-binding refers to the terms of a contract in which the parties are not legally obligated to carry out its terms.
operating agreement (OA)
An operating agreement is a document based on a term sheet, including specifics related to the governance of a company.
option pool
An option pool is the equity set aside for early investors and employees.
options
Options are agreements to sell ownership shares to employees and investors at a set price - usually higher than the current fair market value and lower than the projected fair market value - at a later date.
over allotment option
Over allotment option is the right of investors to exercise the first refusal rights and come along rights of other investors who don’t exercise their own rights.
pari-passu
Pari-passu is a Latin phrase which translates to "with an equal step" or "on equal footing,” In business it means that all lenders must be treated equally in a liquidity event; each receiving an equal share of the proceeds at the same time.
participating preferred stock
Participating preferred stock is a type of preferred stock that gives the holder a specific dividend that is paid before anything is paid to common stockholders. This takes precedence over common stock in the event of a liquidation.
piggyback registration rights
Piggyback registration are rights granted to an investor whereby they may register their unregistered stock upon the company or another investor initiating a registration.
redemption clause
A redemption clause is a contractual provision that allows one party to buy back or redeem an asset or security from
another party at a specified price or under specified conditions. Redemption clauses are commonly used in investment contracts and other types of financial agreements.
In the context of venture capital, a redemption clause may be included in a shareholder agreement or other investment contract to allow the company or its shareholders to buy back the shares of an investor under certain circumstances. For example, a redemption clause may allow the company to buy back shares if the investor violates certain terms of the agreement, or if the company is acquired or goes public.

Valuation Terminology
409a valuation
A 409a valuation is an appraisal that a third party conducts of a startup in order to pinpoint what the fair market value is. Startups get a 409a valuation before they are able to offer investors equity. This is the price at which options are issued to founders, employees and contractors. It also applies to advisors and any other person who receives common stock.
comparable company analysis (CCA)
This is a method of valuation that involves comparing a company to similar companies in the same industry to determine its value. CCA is often used in valuation of publicly traded companies.
discounted cash flow (DCF)
This is a method of valuation that involves projecting future cash flows of a company or asset and discounting them back to their present value. DCF is often used in valuation of companies that have unpredictable earnings or cash flows.
earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization (EBITDA)
This is a financial metric used to measure a company's profitability. EBITDA is often used as a valuation multiple in business valuations.
fair market value
This refers to the price that a willing buyer would pay to a willing
seller for a company or asset in an open market. Fair market value is often used as a benchmark for valuation.
multiples
This refers to the valuation multiples used to determine the value of a startup. Multiples can be based on metrics such as revenue, EBITDA, or number of users.
pre-money valuation
Pre-money valuation is an appraisal startups get before having conducted a fundraising round. The appraisal is likely to be lower than future appraisals due to a lack of liquidity.
post-money valuation
Post-money valuation is an appraisal startups get after having conducted a fundraising round. Because of new liquidity, the appraisal is likely to be higher than past appraisals.

Venture Capital Firm Types
corporate venture capital firms
These firms are subsidiaries of large corporations and invest in startups that have strategic relevance to the parent company's business.
Examples include: Intel Capital, Google Ventures, Comcast Ventures.
early-stage venture capital firms
These firms focus on investing in startups that have already demonstrated some traction in the market, typically at the seed or Series A stage.
Examples include: Andreessen Horowitz, Kleiner Perkins, Bessemer Venture Partners.
generalist venture capital firms
These firms invest in a wide range of industries and stages of development.
Examples include: Accel Partners, Sequoia Capital, Greylock Partners.
growth-stage venture capital firms
These firms specialize in investing in companies that have already achieved significant revenue and user growth, often at the Series B or later stages.
Examples include: Insight Partners, Battery Ventures, NEA.
impact venture capital firms
These firms invest in companies that have a social or environmental impact, in addition to financial returns.
Examples include: Omidyar Network, Acumen, Impact America Fund.
industry-specific venture capital firms
These firms focus on investing in startups in a specific industry or sector, such as healthcare, fintech, or cleantech.
Examples include: Rock Health (healthcare), FinVC (fintech), Clean Energy Venture Group (cleantech).
international venture capital firms
These firms invest in startups located outside of their home country, often with a focus on emerging markets or regions.
Examples include: SoftBank (Japan), Naspers (South Africa), Rakuten Ventures (Japan).
seed-stage venture capital firms
These firms specialize in investing in very early-stage startups, often providing funding for product development and market validation.
Examples include: First Round Capital, Founders Fund.

Venture Capital Terminology
assets under management (AUM)
Assets under management, sometimes called funds under management, refers to the market value of the assets a financial institution has discretion over on behalf of clients or investors.
cram down
In venture capital, a "cram down" refers to a situation where an investor's stake in a company is significantly diluted during a subsequent round of financing. This can happen when a company raises additional funding at a lower valuation than in previous rounds, or when new investors demand a larger stake in the company in exchange for their investment.
deal flow
Deal flow is a term used by angel investors, venture capitalists, private equity investors and investment bankers referring to the quantity of investment opportunities available to them at a given time.
deal lead
A deal lead is the investor who takes charge during a funding round. They lay out the terms for which the investment will occur. Also called the lead investor.
due diligence
Due diligence is when potential investors perform investigation and analysis of a startup, the team, the product, the market, etc. The investigation and analysis may include validating patents,
degrees, customers, credit scores, reference checks, revenue, cash on hand, etc.
limited partnership (LP)
A limited partnership (LP) is when two or more partners go into business together, with the limited partners only liable up to the amount of their investment. It is a form of partnership similar to a general partnership, except that while a general partnership (GP) must have at least two general partners, a limited partnership must have at least one GP and at least one limited partner.
management fee
Management fees are annual fees that a fund charges its limited partners, intended to compensate managers for their time and expertise in managing portfolios.
master limited partnership (MLP)
A master limited partnership (MLP) is a company organized as a publicly-traded partnership, combining private partnership tax advantages with stock liquidity.
power law
The power law is a concept in venture capital that refers to the idea that a small number of investments in a portfolio are responsible for the majority of the returns. In other words, a few "winners" can make up for many "losers" in a venture capital portfolio.
rolling fund
A rolling fund is a type of investment fund structure that allows investors to make recurring, quarterly commitments to a fund.
Rolling funds are a relatively new type of investment vehicle that were enabled by regulatory changes in the United States in 2020.
shareholders' agreement
A shareholders’ agreement is a contract that defines what shareholders owe to a company and what the company, in turn, owes to them.
shares outstanding
Shares outstanding refers to all stock held by a company’s shareholders, including shares held by institutional investors and restricted shares owned by executives.
special purpose vehicle (spv)
SPV stands for Special Purpose Vehicle, which is a legal entity created for a specific purpose or transaction. In the context of investing, an SPV is often used to pool together capital from multiple investors for a single investment opportunity.
venture capitalist (VC)
Venture capitalists (VC) unlike angels, usually invest for a living. They are often interested in startups with traction and proof that an investment will help accelerate their path to financial goals.
venture capital fund
A venture capital fund is a firm that combines multiple investors' capital in order to provide larger funding to startups as they reach their mid-stage fundraising rounds.

Venture Capital Types
corporate venture capital (CVC)
Corporate venture capital (CVC) is the investment of corporate funds directly into smaller startup companies. A large firm takes an equity stake in a startup and may also provide management and marketing expertise to gain a competitive advantage.
fund of funds (FOF)
Funds of funds, often referred to as multi-manager investment, are larger institutional platforms that invest in many different funds. This practice allows institutional investors to get allocations in some funds that they may not have had access to.
early stage financing
Early stage financing refers to the initial capital raised by a company during its early stages of development, typically before the company has generated significant revenue or established a proven business model.
Early stage financing is critical for startups and other emerging companies, as it provides the capital necessary to develop and launch their product or service, build their team, and establish their market presence. Early stage financing may be raised from a variety of sources, including angel investors, venture capital firms, or crowdfunding platforms.
expansion financing
Expansion financing refers to capital raised by a company to support its growth and expansion efforts, typically after it has established a proven business model and generated
some revenue. Expansion financing may be used to finance a variety of activities, such as expanding the company's product line, entering new markets, or investing in research and development.
buyout financing
Buyout financing is a type of financing used to fund the acquisition of a company or business unit by another company, investor group, or management team. Buyouts can take many forms, including management buyouts (MBOs), leveraged buyouts (LBOs), and private equity buyouts.
venture debt
Venture debit is a type of funding in which a startup takes on debt obligations in exchange for cash. It works similarly to a loan.
