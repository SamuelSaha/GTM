Preface
This book is based on a course I created and piloted at Dartmouth and then 
taught for 20 years at Columbia Business School (see Appendix 9A for a 
recent syllabus).
When I began teaching this class in 1990, people largely associated organic 
growth with farming. Since then, they have come to realize its broader 
importance. Indeed, many courses (e.g., on ideation) now go into greater 
detail on parts of it. My hope is that by integrating different aspects, readers 
will find this a useful (and not too taxing) document.
The book is focused on growth for existing companies. However, it is relevant 
for startups as well. The primary difference is that “seed funding” is done 
internally versus externally and the major financial gains (and risks) accrue 
to the company versus the entrepreneur and early round investors.
It is also focused on for-profit companies. Nonetheless, the basics apply to 
nonprofits as well. The main difference is that the metrics of success switch 
from profit, cash flow, and stock price to social impact measures such as 
patients treated and diseases cured for medical conditions, the number fed 
and nutrition for food security, impact on the environment, and the number 
of “converts” for religions.
Stylistically, this book provides a number of templates for developing growth 
strategies through the extensive use of figures. It also is relatively brief based 
on a principle I proposed when I was Executive Director of the Marketing 
Science Institute called “Get to the Point.”

A Practitioner’s Perspective
Investors and shareholders demand growth. Companies demand growth. 
CEOs demand growth. It does not matter whether your marketing title is 
CMO, CGO, CCO, CTO, EVP, SVP, VP, director, manager or a recent 
graduate; in marketing, you are expected to drive growth. When that call to 
find growth comes to you, what do you do? How do you respond to the 
question? How do you get started?
Throughout my career at three major corporations—Dow Chemical, Praxair 
(now Linde), and Dyno Nobel—I was challenged to find and drive profitable, 
organic growth. Professor Don Lehmann of Columbia Business School and 
I met when we were both on the board of the Marketing Science Institute 
(the bridge between academia and business). I shared some of my growth 
challenges with him. While I learned many aspects of growth during my 
MBA at Harvard Business School and early in my career at Dow Chemical, 
it was not until I discussed my growth challenges with Don that I learned 
there could be a systematic way to find growth opportunities. And while 
acquisitions are another way to grow, they are not the focus of this book.
Over many years, we worked together to refine this growth process. Don 
would challenge me again and again with theories and new ideas, and my 
team and I would find practical ways to implement them. Over 14 years, we 
brought many of these growth challenges into his MBA classroom each year 
and the students learned from us as we learned from them. We challenged 
the students to look at the company and identify three “sensible” potential 
growth ideas and one “crazy or wild,” “disruptive,” or “out of the box” idea. 
While we laughed at many of these ideas, there were always some “gems” 
that we worked to refine and develop.

Overall, this systematic growth process works. I successfully implemented 
it at three different companies over the past 30+ years. My journey was 
supported by many excellent marketers who understood the need for growth 
and had the ability to deliver new programs. I am grateful for each one of 
them who fought the growth battle (both internal and external) with me. 
Here’s how you can get started.
Understand the Growth Challenge
First, you must fully understand the growth challenge in front of you. Has 
the growth target been detailed to you in terms of annual dollars or a 
percentage or both? Over the years, prior to the recent spike in inflation, the 
large B2B companies I worked for were typically trying to find high single 
digit growth of 7%–9% of prior year revenue with double digit growth as 
the gold standard. Here is an example equation we used to better understand 
the growth challenge each year in percentages:
Industry Growth Rate (Annual Average) 2%–3%
Expected Annual Price Increase / Decrease 1%–2%
Known New Sales (Signed Contracts) 1%–2%
Profitable New Growth Needed (Solve for it)
X − X%
Company or Business Unit Target for growth 7%–9%
It is up to you (or the Finance Group) whether you add or subtract in any 
foreign exchange (FX) effect. Typically, we kept it straightforward and did 
not include any FX effect.
Sometimes, a simple dollar target works as well. I remember joining a 
company with no defined pipeline of or process for new growth. The first 
dollar target was $50 million per year in new programs. It looked like a 
mountain to me as I started, but soon we met the target. Of course, what 
happens with successful companies when you meet one target, you get a 
bigger one. As we were successful in identifying and driving new growth

The Systematic Growth Process
The systematic approach to growth involves three main aspects focusing on 
new products, branding, and customers. New product development, branding, 
and customer management are all related, but we will look at the key aspects 
of each one. To be successful, a new program will often touch on each of 
these aspects.
New Product Development
There is always opportunity for new product development. What purity, size, 
package, or service is missing? There are many techniques highlighted by 
others to find new growth ideas. Look around the company and take an 
inventory of new products being discussed or under development in R&D, 
technical service, regional businesses, or in the field.
We live in an age of increasing technology. Take a look at what value 
technology adds or could add to your product. Digitization of your product 
or product experience is valuable.

On the other hand, can you simplify your product to remove unused, 
undervalued, or underperforming features or technology? Can that 
simplification expand your product’s appeal or reduce your costs?
Branding
Brand extensions are a good place to look for growth. When looking at 
growth of a brand, or more importantly growth through new brands, you 
need to ask if you have permission from your customers to extend the brand 
in the direction you are considering. Does the growth program align with 
what customers expect of you? Do you have the capability to do it well? Can 
you build the competencies you need for the new program in a timely manner? 
The Chapter 5 on growth through brand extensions or new brands will focus 
on this.
Customer Management
Customers come from three places: retained, acquired, or noncustomers that 
are converted from another source. Reinforce that customers made a good 
decision choosing your new product or service. Often follow-up or training 
is useful with new and existing customers. If customers left you or did not 
choose your new growth product, then look for patterns or trends to explain 
their behavior. Look beyond price and ask why customers defect or do not 
choose you at all.
Customers can tell you much about your products and provide ideas for new 
ones. They may not always know their future needs, but they often 
understand today’s product better than you do. Take a deep look at quality 
complaints, returns, and service errors. What issue are they having while 
using your product? Take a look at your channel to market. Listen to them 
about needs and issues. Include the right lead customers in your new product 
developments.

Responding to the Statement: You’re not  
Growing Fast Enough
Companies, leaders, and shareholders are not patient. Growth is hard. Every 
marketer must be prepared to respond when told they are not growing fast 
enough. Often growth programs take twists along the path to success which 
require more time or investment and you fall behind plan. There are three 
possible answers to the issue of not growing fast enough:
Thorough Study—the problem is this takes time and resources
Quick Study—the problem is what to study and accuracy
Quick Thinking—need questions to ask
Let’s examine some quick thinking questions you can ask your team to 
accelerate the growth programs. Look for what you are missing.
Why are you not doing better than the competition?
What is the competition doing better than you?
Do you clearly understand the decision process of target customers?
Did you pick the correct lead customers?
Based on early learning, can you make the new product better?
Are there unanticipated switching costs or risks for the user?
Did you clearly articulate the value or benefit for the customer?

Other Thoughts on Growth
No matter what size your growth challenge is, you should get started on 
building your growth portfolio today. Structured creativity helps generate

Why Focus on (Marketing-Driven Organic) 
Growth?
For a start-up, growth is obviously critical. For a large publicly held company, 
the most obvious reason to grow is to increase its value. As the principle

 • A McKinsey Survey showed that what CEOs most wanted from

 • A McCann-Erickson Survey found that the greatest challenge was

 • A 2011 Conference Board survey found that business growth was the

service companies.
 • A 2019 CMO survey reported that 37.9% listed growth as the top 
challenge and another 28.9% listed it as second or third.
 • A 2021 survey by McKinsey reported that 72% of CEOs were relying 
on marketing to drive growth.

The Value of Growth
For the sake of simplicity, consider the value of a firm with a constant growth 
rate. Assuming that earnings ( E) each year stay the same (i.e., the growth 
rate g is 0), the value of the firm is:
  E /  
[  1   −  (  1 / 1   +   d )   ]     =   E    (   (  1   +   d )   / d )    
Where d is the discount rate (cost of capital) and E is net revenue (gross 
margin). For a 10% discount rate, this formula would justify a Price/Earnings 
ratio of (1.1/0.1) = 11. (The formula reduces to E/d if revenue begins next 
year, i.e., that of a perpetuity.)
Now assume that the firm grows at a constant rate g (where g is less than 
the discount rate d). Then the value would be:
  E   /  [  1   −    (   (  1   +   g )     /  (  1   +   d )   )   ]     =   E    (   (  1   +   d )   /  (  d   −   g )   )  .

Nonorganic growth (i.e., growth by acquisitions) is problematic for two 
reasons:
 1. The seller knows more about the business than the buyer. That means 
the seller’s willingness to sell often leads the buyer to overpay.
 2. The costs of integrating firms’ operating systems, customer relations, 
and cultures are often underestimated.

Can Acquisitions be Profitable?
Yes, if the acquiring firm has the assets and will to expand faster than the 
acquired firm (Mishra and Slotegraaf, 2013). This is more likely to occur 
when the acquirer has stronger marketing capabilities, is acquiring a higher-
quality company, or actually can achieve cost savings (Wiles, Morgan, and 
Rego, 2012). It also helps if the acquirer has strong product development 
capabilities (Sorescu, Chandy, and Prabhu, 2007) and not if the companies 
are either too similar (e.g., Sears, Kmart) or too different (e.g., eBay, Skype). 
In other words, mergers can work if the acquirer can increase the organic 
growth rate of the acquired firm (which is not the typical case). Therefore, 
this book is focused on achieving organic growth, i.e., taking an existing 
business and expanding it, rather than creating one from scratch (although 
the techniques presented here apply well to that also) or “financial engineering.”
What about Just Marketing Harder?
An instinct some marketers have is to use the classic 4-Ps, in particular price 
and promotion (advertising), to try to drive profitable revenue growth. The 
position taken here is that it is extremely unlikely to be successful. For 
example, using price promotions to increase sales causes competitors to match, 
leading to lower margins and training many customers to only buy on sale 
(e.g., Lands’ End merchandise is usually 30%–40% off and Pepsi and Coke 
products are alternately on promotion). More broadly, so-called persistence 
modeling shows that rarely is a permanent change driven by changing the 
marketing mix (i.e., customers attracted by it do not become loyal customers).
What about Advertising Spending?
Absent something new to say (e.g., about a new product or significant new 
use for an old one), advertising elasticities tend to be 0.1 or less for existing 
brands (versus 0.3 for “new” information ads).

 2. It helps attract, retain, and energize employees. Being on a “winning

When not to Focus on Growth
Of course, growth is not always the goal. In my master’s program, we had 
a case involving a business that had an opportunity to grow substantially. I, 
like most of the class, saw it as a no-brainer to take it—and argued so 
vociferously. At that point, the professor told me and one other student to 
leave the room and that when we returned, if we couldn’t convince a majority 
of the class that the company should not take the opportunity, our grades 
would be lowered one letter. We then realized that it was (1) a family 
company, (2) with an old owner/operator who had no obvious successor or 
family member interested in the business, who (3) could live comfortably 
on what the business was earning. Thus, our argument, which the class 
bought, was “why try to grow and risk a comfortable retirement?” More 
broadly, growth is not necessarily the goal for a family or “hobby”/part-time 
business.
The other broad category of situations where growth is not “the” goal is 
when there are serious negative social consequences (sometimes referred to 
as externalities). These include damage to the environment, health concerns 
for employees and others, and economic disruption to employees and other 
businesses. While “creative destruction” is to be expected, at some point, 
these concerns outweigh the benefits of profitable organic growth. Concerns 
related to how to grow will be explicitly discussed in the chapters on Selection 
and Implementation.

Introduction
Obviously, a key to deciding whether to proceed with a growth strategy is 
to estimate the level of sales (revenue) it will achieve. More specifically, 
consider the following three questions:
 1. What level will sales eventually attain? (i.e., market penetration/
potential)
 2. How will they get there? (i.e., what will sales be over time)
 3. What do we have to do to get sales there? (i.e., how can we influence 
the pattern of sales) and how much will it cost to do it?
The third question is crucial and focuses on implementation. Before 
addressing this, it is useful to get a basic idea of the answers to the first two 
questions (i.e., generate a crude forecast).
There are two basic approaches to forecasting, qualitative (intuition/judgment 
based) or more thorough consideration, which consists of carefully evaluating 
the concept, firm capabilities, marketplace conditions, and quantitative 
analysis. The more qualitative approaches are covered in some detail in 
Chapter 7 on selecting growth options. This chapter focuses on more 
quantitative approaches.

Estimating Potential
A key aspect of demand estimation is assessing a new offering’s potential. 
This is typically done both subjectively and quantitatively.
Subjective Judgment
Personal Opinion. Whoever created or is “in charge” of an innovation is 
generally knowledgeable about it and its intended uses. Unfortunately, the 
creator may also have hopes and visions that can bias their thinking. In the 
extreme, the “forecast” may be the number needed to justify the project, 
which is more wishful thinking or political than a real forecast (even if it is 
frequently used).
Structured Thinking. One sensible way to forecast makes use of a sequential 
approach that begins with a broad potential market and then examines how 
that market will translate into sales. For a business-to-business product, this 
approach might look like the following:
(Number of businesses for which the product or service is relevant) * (Percent 
likely to purchase) * (Average purchase rate)
Similarly, for a business-to-consumer product, the logic often is:
(Number of households in target market) * (Percent likely to purchase) * 
(Average purchase rate)
Expert Opinion(s). This approach assumes that the expert’s expertise is 
relevant and tends to hold true more often for type A patterns (minor 
innovations) than for type C (radical ones).

Generating Growth Ideas
Generating good growth ideas is often the “fun” part of the process but 
can also be frustrating and result in a fairly limited set of options. The 
purpose of the chapter is to highlight some useful ways to structure the 
process, many of which are thought of as ways to foster creative thinking. 
While many of these methods are oriented toward new products, they also 
apply more broadly (e.g., to brand, customer, and channel-based growth 
ideas).
Methods for generating growth ideas fall into two basic categories: market 
input-driven and ideation.
Market/Customer Input-Driven Methods
This category includes the use of both classic market research methods and 
passive/observational ones.
Surveys
Standard marketing research methods that ask about goals, attribute 
importance (e.g., via conjoint analysis), and satisfaction (or lack thereof) can 
produce useful insights. For example, Courtyard by Marriott was structured 
based on input from a conjoint analysis. Simply asking about what would 
improve a product can produce useful input.
Importantly, who you ask is as important as what you ask them. Asking 
current/satisfied customers is likely to produce fairly minor changes (they

Listening to Customer Comments, Suggestions, and Complaints
Both direct-to-the-company and those encountered by frontline employees/
salespeople, complaints and suggestions can produce useful (and of course 
many not-so-useful) ideas.
Social Media Monitoring
The Internet provides a rich source for discovering what customers want.

Observation
Observing what customers do to accomplish their objectives when using 
your offering allows you to see where the “pain points” are.
Looking for “Duct-Tape” Solutions
Out of need or interest, individuals often create crude (“duct-tape”) products 
and procedures (like the TV character MacGyver) for accomplishing tasks. 
An intriguing version of this is exploring “bottom of the pyramid” inventions 
which can then be adopted and marketed. In general, for every problem,

For a growth idea to succeed, it needs to be “adopted” by all the relevant 
parties. This obviously involves customers but also company employees 
(executives, salespeople, etc.), channels (both suppliers and distribution 
channels unless the innovation is strictly direct B-to-C), regulators, etc. Here 
we focus on customer adoption. We discuss adoption by others in Chapter 8 
on implementation.
Implementing a growth strategy involves getting people to behave in different 
(innovative) ways. However, humans are, in general, not innovative. Rather, 
they cling to their past way of doing things. This is part evolutionary biology 
(the “I survived so far this way so I’m staying here” reaction), part nostalgia, 
and part a reaction to the often-bewildering array of options available.
Of course, inertia is not inevitable. Some individuals are more prone to 
innovate due to either abnormal inquisitiveness or perpetual dissatisfaction. 
In addition, there are circumstances, such as desperation (“any port in a 
storm,” “I’ve got nothing to lose”) or boredom, which encourage people to 
try new things. For the most part, however, individuals tend to be somewhat 
satisfied with their lives and hence resistant to change. For example, potential 
customers for a new product have been getting along without it and so are 
not necessarily receptive to a new one, no matter how good the technical 
support data is or how enthusiastically you promote it.

Brand-Driven Growth
Branding is an integral part of most marketing. The word is related to the 
old Norse word Brandr, which means “to burn.” This was a common practice 
in the United States in the 1800s as a means to identify stolen property 
(cattle). Since then, it has taken on other roles. The person who was in charge 
of the brand at GE when Welch was CEO suggested that a brand had three 
roles: simplifying choice, promising quality, and engendering trust. To these, 
luxury marketers would add giving prestige. Indeed, “building your brand” 
now includes personal branding (which, sadly, seems to be a fetish of high 
school students and frequent users of social media).
Brand as Identifier
Logo
It helps to have a distinctive identifying mark to create a brand. Paul Revere 
placed a mark on the bottom of his silverware. Unfortunately, most logos 
you can think of will be similar to that of someone else, leading to issues 
with trademark infringement. Fortunately, logos rarely generate sales. Nike’s 
ubiquitous swoosh was created by a Portland State design student and used 
not because it was strongly liked but because it seemed better than the other 
options at the time. Nike’s early success was built on its reputation as a 
quality running shoe used by Steve Prefontaine, the University of Oregon 
runner. To paraphrase a once popular line, “It’s the product, stupid.”

Leveraging the Brand/Brand Architecture: Brand 
Extensions
A common growth strategy is to use a brand’s strength to expand to other 
products and categories. Traditionally, introducing more products in the same 
category (e.g., a different flavor or size) is called a line extension, and 
introducing products in a different category is called a brand extension. There 
is a continuum from relatively close (and, therefore, safer but with less 
potential) to distant (and, therefore, riskier although often with a bigger 
potential upside). In pursuing this route, the key questions are (a) where could 
you extend? and (b) which possibility is the most promising? One long-term 
consideration is that only expanding within a relatively small span of options 
may “typecast” the brand such that later on it may not have “permission” to 
expand more broadly (Parker et al., 2018).
Examples of consumer product extensions include Ocean Spray Cranberry’s 
attempt to move beyond a condiment for Thanksgiving dinner, the various 
beers (e.g., Bud) to enter the seltzer market, WeatherTech (known for its car 
mats) to pet products, and Pepsi Raw and Coke Lite to the healthier segment 
of the soda market. Other examples are IBM’s move to consulting and cloud 
computing and Nike’s mostly but not always successful extensions to clothing 
and sports equipment. Entertainment spin-offs are also extensions (e.g., Law 
and Order, CSI, and NCIS) and Marriott and Hilton have developed (and 
sometimes purchased) a broad line of hotel options (e.g., Courtyard by 
Marriott).

intention does not necessarily translate into an actual purchase; events can 
intervene and distract the person before making the purchase. Similarly, 
customers may encounter unfavorable conditions at the point of purchase (the 
“moment of truth”) such as badly displayed or damaged merchandise, a rude 
salesperson, or complex transaction (e.g., “please read the following eight-
page agreement and sign below”). They may also simply decide not to make 
a “snap decision” or choose to “sleep on it” and “pause” their journey. During 
the pause, they can reconsider or simply forget about their planned purchase.
The “pause and contemplate” phase is typically overlooked. Nonetheless, it 
is an important one where purchase intentions are either reinforced or die. 
Of course, a critical branch, not included in Figure 6.1, is the decision not 
to buy, which can be made at any stage in the journey.
Another rarely considered aspect is the time between purchase and use. Some 
of this may occur because of out-of-stock conditions or variable delivery 
time (even Amazon Prime is not instantaneous). During this time, a 
customer’s needs may change such that the person never actually uses their 
purchase or at least uses it under circumstances where its advantages are 
less clear (e.g., when it stops raining or snowing).
Use generally causes a re-evaluation. In addition to its implications for future 
consideration and future purchases, which can range from deciding to never 
use or purchase it again to making it a routine (automatic) order product, the 
evaluation can influence recommendations. These recommendations can 
either help sell the product to others or, in the case of negative word-of-mouth, 
make sales considerably more difficult.

Detecting “At-Risk” Customers
There are four general warning signs of defection:
 1. Announcements
People frequently “announce” their intention directly to a company or 
make it clear that they will not be renewing a contract or demonstrate 
a decreased willingness to pay for it. They also announce it via word-
of-mouth—often through social media. So-called “sentiment analysis” 
of tweets, Facebook posts, etc. (including changes in how often 
something is featured as well as direct comments) can highlight both 
individuals at risk of defection and more general population tendencies 
to do so. They may also feel harassed due to overly frequent contact 
(e.g., by an airline or online retailer), and ask to be taken off a contact 
list or simply identify the source as junk mail.
 2. Change in use
Retention depends on a certain level of inertia on the part of customers. 
Changes in use suggest they may be in more of a mover/switcher mode. 
These changes include the obvious (reduced total use/purchases, 
increased time between purchases) as well as more subtle changes in 
the pattern of purchases (e.g., reduced regularity).
 3. Reduced attention/care
Valued possessions inspire efforts to ensure their continued functioning. 
Service contracts, scheduled and unscheduled maintenance, and general 
care (e.g., waxing/detailing a car) are signs of attachment.
 4. Friends’ (or similar others’) defections
Just as friends’ behavior can encourage adoption, when friends disadopt 
something, it can encourage or at least foretell future disadoption in 
their “neighborhood” among those they are connected to. In addition, 
when people who are similar to a customer (even those who are not 
“connected” to them) disadopt, this may signal a fundamental reason 
has emerged for disadoption.
Monitoring such warning signs is strongly advised.

Dealing with Unprofitable Customers
The most obvious way to deal with unprofitable customers is to stop doing 
business with them. However, this approach is frequently infeasible (e.g., 
due to laws and regulations as well as negative publicity).
One consideration is that customers who produce negative net revenue in the 
short run may become profitable in the long run (consider young investors 
or passive investors who, as they age, may become candidates for fee-based 
financial advisors). Also, since unprofitable customers may make up 50% or 
more of the customer base, dropping them will lead to shifting some of their 
costs to the remaining customers making some of them also appear 
unprofitable. This will lead to dropping more of them which will in turn 
shift those costs to the ones that are left and so on, eventually leading to a 
very bad ending.
It may also be mutually beneficial to move them to a different category. For 
example, one bank sent a letter to unprofitable customers suggesting a 
different service which was profitable and which saved the customer money. 
Thousands opted for this win–win option.
Another approach is based on reducing retention/service cost (which can run 
the risk of defection). One way to do this is by grouping rather than serving 
customers one at a time, thus reducing service cost.
If a “divorce” seems like the best option, an amicable one is best. This can 
be facilitated by moving customers to another (desirable) firm.
The last option is to “fire” a customer. One car dealer in Texas did this 
because in spite of being a customer whose Customer Lifetime Value from

Program Evaluation Based on CLV
CLV provides a way to evaluate potential marketing programs. This can be 
done directly when the impact on acquisition, retention, and margin is fairly

program cost by the average CLV of new customers it will attract and/or 
those who will increase the margin they provide, one gets a breakeven number 
of customers needed. This can be a large and sobering number as well as a 
logical argument to share with the program’s proponents.

Aggregate Customer Value  
and Firm Value
The value of a firm as a going business is largely the value of cash flow from 
current and future customers minus required “fixed” costs (R&D, marketing, 
and infrastructure) to serve customers. Other assets, such as employees, 
intellectual property, and financial assets, are also relevant but tend to be 
less consequential than the flow of revenue from customers. This concept 
was demonstrated by Gupta, Lehmann, and Stuart (2004) in response to the 
bubble in value of many stocks in the late 1990s (Internet 1.0) and has since 
been extended by others (e.g., McCarthy and Fader, 2018).
Valuing firms based on customers can be approximated by the following five 
steps:
 1. Project future cohorts (acquisition)
These generally follow an S-shaped pattern and can be projected using 
the Bass Model or logistic regression.
 2. Project future margins
 3. Compute the total CLV (customer equity) of each cohort (current plus 
future)
 4. Discount the total CLV back to the present
 5. Add up the present values across cohorts
Much of this can be done based on public documents. In their paper, Gupta, 
Lehmann, and Stuart’s (2004) estimates were reasonably close to the market 
caps of Ameritrade, Capital One (at the time not an e-commerce company), and 
E*TRADE. However, the method underestimated the value of eBay and Amazon. 
The underestimation of eBay is explainable because the analysis only considered 
one side (the buyers) of a two-sided market and didn’t consider revenue from

---

## Page 133

108 Customer-Based Growth
sellers). The underestimation of the value of Amazon stems from the fact that 
at the time, Amazon’s primary source of revenue was book sales. The expansion 
of their business model to selling basically everything vastly increased both the 
number of customers and the net revenue per customer. This highlights a caution: 
using current and past data does not capture the option value of a successful 
major expansion of a firm’s business model. On the other hand, future projections 
are often overblown and overly optimistic, leading to a decrease in stock price

Evaluating Growth Options: Screening Tools
The “gold standard” for evaluating growth options is projected discounted 
profits (or cash flow). There are three problems with this approach. First, 
they are difficult to estimate so that it is hard to apply to one, let alone a large 
number of options. Second, if done realistically, taking into account both 
different scenarios and statistical uncertainty, the range of outcomes is large 
as shown in Chapter 2, Figure 2.8. Third, firms are increasingly including 
social impacts (e.g., the “triple” bottom line of profit, people, and planet) for 
which a quantitative estimate of the latter two is very difficult (e.g., what is 
the cost of lithium mining for batteries, fires caused by them, disposal, etc.?). 
Therefore, at least for the purpose of initial screening, a more qualitative 
rating scale-type approach is useful.
It is not hard to come up with lots of options ranging from minor tweaks to 
discontinuous innovations. In many/most situations, it should be obvious that 
(a) you can’t pursue all of them, and (b) you can’t lay out detailed plans and 
work out financial projections for all of them. Fortunately, at this stage, these 
are not needed. What is needed is an initial screening method. This drives the 
need to find a fairly simple method of screening for likely winners and losers.
Many different screening criteria can be used. Here is one set based on the 
answers to 10 questions.

To facilitate comparisons across concepts, it is often useful to rate each

made by sequentially considering each question, starting with the most 
critical, or by creating a score across the questions by either summing them 
directly or applying weights to the scores reflecting how important they are 
and then summing them.
The first question is the most basic. Will anyone buy it? More specifically, 
how large will demand be if we do it “right”?
The second question is what will its impact be on customers? This assesses 
whether their lives will be improved by its use, taking into account both 
benefits and side effects/unintended consequences.
The third question is about implementation. Even if customers would buy it, 
that does us no good if we can’t produce it. Here, we break down capabilities 
on five dimensions: design, make, finance, market, and manage.
The fourth question is what will its impact be on society and the environment 
in the short and long run? This is an increasingly important consideration 
as evidenced by movements like conscious capitalism and the triple bottom 
line (profits, people, and planet).

The fifth question addresses whether other parties will be upset or try to 
block our doing it. This depends on the answer to the fourth question, 
including formal legal structures and more general public reaction as well 
as likely competitive response. Importantly, it also includes current customers 
who may, for example, not be happy if you materially change your current 
offering or alter a brand’s image.
The sixth question, whose answer depends on the answers to the previous 
questions, is an attempt to get a handle on the size of the opportunity (i.e., 
market sizing). This addresses the issue of whether sales will be 1,000, 10,000, 
or 100,000 units, and results in a very crude assessment of market potential.
The seventh question is whether the firm will obtain additional benefits from 
this. These can include more loyal customers or positive word-of-mouth (e.g., 
from an innovative product), employee satisfaction, and/or learning (e.g., 
from an initial venture in a country or technology).
The eighth question assesses other factors that could impact the result, for 
example, sensitivity to economic cycles or diversion of effort from other 
parts of the business, such as that due to the reassignment of key personnel.
The ninth factor is financial consequences (i.e., revenue, profit, and cash 
flow). For initial screening, these can be crude estimates (i.e., accurate to 
one or at most two decimal places). As the number of options is winnowed 
down, more precise projections become more useful. Importantly, financial 
consequences are driven by the answers to the previous eight questions.
The final factor is how sure (confident) we are in our evaluations. Uncertainty 
is normally thought of as bad, but it can indicate a chance for a large upside 
as well. Whether variation is good or bad depends on a firm’s risk tolerance.
Of course, not all of the 10 questions may be equally important, so a weighted 
overall score may be needed. The weights depend on the needs of the 
company.

Electronic markets have been used to predict the outcome of political 
elections (the Iowa Election Markets), movies attendance (MSX.com),  
reactions to concepts (such as crossover vehicles), and the value of specific

multiple periods. If a buyer thinks that the concept has a 70% chance of 
success, the expected value of it is (70% * $100) = $70 and they would 
buy if the price were lower and sell if it were higher. The eventual market 
price then tends toward the probability the concept will succeed. 
Alternatively, a prediction market can be used to predict actual sales.

What Happened and Why
One thing that is interesting is how “little things” often determined whether 
the concepts succeeded or failed. Take a look at these results:
Idea 1 in 2005: NatureWash—Not successful; the incumbent technology, 
chlorine wash, is well-proven, easy to use, and inexpensive. It is still used 
today.
Idea 2 in 2006: Controlled Atmosphere Stunning —Successful; the 
most hazardous job in a turkey plant is removing live turkeys from a truck 
and hanging them for processing and this technology eliminated employee 
hazards.
Idea 3 in 2007: Climate Change Opportunities —Not successful; in 
2007, climate change was coming onto the scene as important, but we did 
not know exactly what was important, how to measure positive results, 
and the timing was bad because the end of 2007–2009 marked “The Great 
Recession.”
Idea 4 in 2010—2012: H 2 for Fuel Cell Forklifts —Successful; the 
project initially focused on a subsegment of fuel cell market (forklifts). 
While it was a small market, it proved the technology and is still growing.

technological marvel in its time, but high purity cylinders were paid for 
via credit card from the expense budget and the equipment was considered 
a capital expense, and the capital process was bureaucratic and dollars

A Practitioner’s View
This section focuses on some of the processes that Scott as a practitioner 
found useful in driving growth in three organizations.
Sometimes new product growth can be confused with ideation. New product 
growth involves ideation, but there are several other steps and some specific 
areas where you can focus to find organic growth opportunities.
What you often need to do is recharge the organization to drive a culture of 
collaboration and innovation. To accomplish change, you may need to realign 
resources toward growth. If growth is a part-time job, then you will get 
limited growth. The pressing day-to-day product activities will always take 
precedence over the slower, growth activities. If you have the budget to 
dedicate resources to growth, you will have the greatest success in driving 
it. If one or more people in your organization get up each day with only 
growth on their mind and they are evaluated in terms of growth, then they 
will almost always find ways to grow your business. However, a dedicated 
growth organization is not always possible, so here are some ways to stimulate 
growth with less than fully dedicated resources.
One technique that has worked well is establishing formal product teams 
that meet either monthly or quarterly with assigned members as follows:
 • Business Sponsor
 • Team Leader (often the product manager)
 • Cross functional members from Sales, R&D, Engineering, 
Manufacturing, Supply Chain, Finance, etc.
 • Channel Representative
Having the correct group members with a growth mindset and a driven 
growth sponsor are keys to success.

Growth Syllabus
Companies in general and marketers in particular are under pressure to 
produce two main things: effective/productive spending and “real” (organic) 
revenue growth. This course addresses the second issue, organic growth. 
More specifically, it looks at three main (and interrelated) paths to growth: 
new products, brands, and customers. Participants play the role of growth 
consultants/Chief Growth Officers. The course is designed for people 
interested in management, consulting, and entrepreneurship as well as 
marketing.
After laying out some basic “algebra” of growth, each of three growth paths 
(new product, customer, and brand-based) are examined.
Participants:
 a) Discuss standard cases and two live cases (current situations).
 b) Explore specific methods designed to spur creative thinking.
 c) Present examples of successful and unsuccessful growth strategies 
drawn from current or past practice (i.e., best and worst practices).
 d) Draw practical implications from carefully selected relevant academic 
literature, much of it Columbia related, and present a brief synopsis of 
one article in class.
 e) Generate growth options for a specific firm as the basis for the final
