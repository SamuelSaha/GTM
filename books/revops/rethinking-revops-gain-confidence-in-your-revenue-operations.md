Revenue Operations
What is RevOps?
Revenue Operations (RevOps) is a holistic approach to managing the
entire revenue process, from lead generation to customer retention.
It involves aligning and integrating the efforts of different teams and
departments, such as sales, marketing, partners, and customer
success, to optimize revenue growth. RevOps focuses on creating a
seamless experience for customers and streamlining internal
processes to drive efficiency and improve scalability.
What is the RevOps model?
The RevOps model involves a set of consistent actions that a
company can take across various functions to facilitate revenue
growth. This model guides internal operations and enhances revenue
generation by improving coordination among different teams.
The RevOps model can be customized according to a company’s
revenue objectives, business model, and operational plan, among
other factors. One of the most fundamental aspects of a RevOps
model is simplifying tools and data across departments by
consolidating operational and enablement responsibilities. This
unified approach enables different units and teams to work together
cohesively, treating the organization as a single entity with shared
goals.
Certain essential attributes typically form part of a RevOps model.
For instance, most include end-to-end process design supporting the
complete customer life cycle, seamless interconnected workflows
spanning multiple functions, data, systems, and visibility into
revenue process execution and outcomes. However, it’s important to
note that these elements only provide a strategic starting point to

Bow-Tie Funnel Framework
What is the bow-tie funnel in
RevOps?
The bow-tie funnel is a RevOps model that illustrates the complete
customer life cycle, encompassing interactions from prospecting to
customer success, retention, and expansion. Unlike the traditional
sales funnel, this model includes acquisition and retention stages.
RevOps teams employ this comprehensive approach to align sales,
marketing, partner, and customer success efforts, optimize the
customer journey, and generate maximum revenue. The bow-tie
funnel provides data-driven insights for ongoing improvements and
fosters cross-functional collaboration, which results in enhanced
customer experiences, reduced churn, and increased lifetime value.
The diagram of the bow-tie funnel, originally from Winning by
Design, has been modified to include an Advocacy stage that comes
after the growing stage.

## Page 16

The bow-tie funnel is a visual representation of the customer
journey with seven main stages:
1. Awareness/Prospect: This is the stage in which potential
customers become aware of a company’s products or
services. The goal is to attract attention and generate interest
in the company’s offerings.
2. Education/MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead): Once
potential customers know about a company’s products or
services, they may become interested in learning more.
Effective education programs provide information and teach
potential customers about the company’s offerings, features,
and benefits.
3. Selection / SQL ( Sales Qualified Lead): After potential
customers have learned about a company’s products or
services, they must evaluate their options and select the best
fit. The goal is to help the customer make an informed
decision by providing the necessary information and support.
4. Onboarding/ Commit or Net New: At the onboarding
stage, the customer purchases and uses the product or
service. Onboarding services should ensure a smooth process
and provide the customer with the necessary resources and
support to get started.
5. Using/Impacting/Live: This is the stage where the
customer is actively using the product or service and
experiencing its impact. Organizations should strive to
maximize customer satisfaction and address any issues
during this stage.
6. Growing/MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue): At some
point, many customers will look to expand their product or
service usage and potentially add more. This stage provides
an opportunity to upsell or cross-sell additional products or
services and continue to provide value to the customer.
7. LTV/Lifetime Value/Advocacy: At the LTV (Lifetime

---

## Page 17

Value/Advocacy) stage, customers have been with the
company for an extended period and have generated
substantial revenue. The primary goal is to foster customer
loyalty and transform satisfied customers into brand
advocates who can promote the product to their community
or recommend it as a preferred vendor in their next company.
Here are the measuring factors for each stage of the bow-tie funnel:
1. Lead-to-opportunity: Measures the percentage of leads
that become sales opportunities by dividing the number of
qualified opportunities by the total leads generated.
2. Opportunity-to-close: Measures the percentage of sales
opportunities closed as deals by dividing closed deals by total
sales opportunities.
3. Onboarding retention: Measures the percentage of
customers completing onboarding and continuing to use the
product/service by dividing the number of completed
onboardings by total customers.
4. Revenue expansion: Measures increased revenue from
existing customers by comparing revenue in a given period to
the previous period.
5. Word-of-mouth net new: Measures new customers
acquired through word-of-mouth marketing by tracking
referrals and other marketing efforts.
6. Win rate: Measures the percentage of closed deals won by
dividing deals won by total deals.
7. Sales cycle: Measures time from initial lead contact to
closing a deal by measuring the time between contact and
closure.
8. Time to live: Measures time from onboarding completion to
product/service use by measuring the time between
completion and use.
9. Contract term: Measures the time agreed for a customer to

What is the double diamond

The double diamond is a popular problem-solving framework
emphasizing empathy, creativity, and iterative prototyping to
generate user-centric solutions. It consists of two diamonds, each
with four phases: Discover, Define, Develop, and Deliver.
The first diamond focuses on problem identification and solution
exploration through research and brainstorming, while the second
emphasizes prototyping and testing. The double diamond provides a
structured approach to problem-solving that encourages exploration
and often leads to creative results.

Practical Exercise: How Might We (HMW)...
What is “How might we?”
“How might we” (HMW) is a design thinking technique used to
reframe a problem into a question that inspires creative solutions. It
involves taking the original problem statement and rephrasing it as a
question that begins with “How might we?” By focusing on the “how
might we” question, designers can approach the problem with a
sense of possibility and opportunity rather than feeling constrained
by limitations or obstacles. The HMW technique is a simple but

RevOps real-time monitoring and testing is continuously tracking and
testing revenue-generating functions to ensure they operate
efficiently and effectively. This process involves monitoring key
performance indicators (KPIs) and testing data accuracy and
completeness. It allows organizations to detect issues with revenue-
generating functions as soon as they arise, make informed decisions,
optimize processes, and improve the customer experience. Real-time
monitoring and testing is a powerful tool for RevOps teams that
want to document their revenue system while continuously testing,
being proactive instead of reactive.

RevOps monitoring and validation should be used by organizations
that want to ensure their revenue-generating functions are
performing optimally and contributing to the organization’s growth.
It is especially useful when an organization’s revenue growth has
stalled or is not meeting expectations.

Not all Tests and Scenarios are Created Equal
Not all test cases and scenarios are equally important or relevant.
Depending on the product or service being developed, some test
cases may be more critical than others. For example, functional tests
that ensure the core features of a product work as intended will
likely be more important than tests for minor cosmetic issues.
Similarly, test scenarios that cover edge cases or high-risk areas of
the product may receive higher priority than those that cover less
critical functionality.

---

## Page 71

Additionally, the context in which a product or service will be used
may also influence which test cases and scenarios are considered
most important. For example, a product used in a safety-critical
industry, such as health care or aviation, will require a different set
of tests and scenarios than a product intended for consumer use.
Identifying and prioritizing the most important test cases and
scenarios is essential to ensure that a product or service is
thoroughly tested and any issues are identified and addressed before
it is released to the public. This is why most organizations have a
test strategy that defines what test cases and scenarios need to be
tested at what stage of product development and which are high-
priority.
Evergreen vs. one-time campaigns
Evergreen revenue operations testing and monitoring refers to
monitoring and analyzing the performance of key operations
expected to generate ongoing income. This process helps ensure
these operations run smoothly and efficiently and deliver the desired
results. It may include monitoring metrics like lead generation, sales
conversion rates, customer lifetime value, and customer churn.
On the other hand, one-time monitoring in revenue operations refers
to testing and analyzing the performance of a specific, time-limited
revenue-generating initiative or project. This might include the
launch of a new product or the execution of a targeted sales
campaign. The insights gained through monitoring can inform future
initiatives and projects and adjust the current campaign to optimize
its performance.
