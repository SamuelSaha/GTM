WHAT IS REVENUE

evenue Operations (RevOps) has become a key framework over
the past 15 years, transforming how businesses align their sales,
marketing, and customer success teams. Originally developed to
address inefficiencies caused by siloed departments, RevOps has

The Evolution of Business Silos

THE HISTORY OF REVOPS
evenue Operations (RevOps) might seem like a relatively recent
framework, but its roots stretch back over 15 years, evolving to
meet the ever-changing needs of businesses. To understand why
RevOps has become such a critical part of modern business
operations, it's important to explore its historical development. This
chapter traces the evolution of organisational structures and the
operational challenges that led to the rise of RevOps.
The Early Days of Sales and Marketing Silos
For decades, sales and marketing operated in silos, each focusing on
their individual priorities. Sales teams were dedicated to closing
deals, while marketing teams concentrated on generating leads. The
lack of alignment between these two departments often resulted in
inefficiencies and friction. Here are some of the common issues:
Sales Complaints: Sales teams frequently criticised
marketing for providing low-quality leads that didn’t
translate into closed deals.
Marketing Frustration: On the other side, marketing
often felt that sales wasn’t effectively following up on
the leads they generated.
This disconnection was especially problematic in larger
organisations, where different teams relied on different tools and
metrics, resulting in a fragmented customer experience. These silos
weren’t just frustrating for internal teams—they negatively impacted

---

## Page 14

customers by creating a disjointed journey from initial engagement
through to long-term retention.
The Rise of Sales Operations
By the 1990s, as businesses expanded their sales efforts, the need
for specialised roles to support these functions became clear. Thus,
Sales Operations (SalesOps) emerged. SalesOps was primarily
tasked with optimising sales processes, managing CRM systems, and
ensuring that the sales team had the right tools and data to
succeed. While this helped improve sales efficiency, it didn't solve
the broader problem of misalignment with marketing and customer
success.
Marketing Operations Joins the Fray
In the early 2000s, Marketing Operations (MarketingOps) developed
as a way to optimise marketing functions. MarketingOps teams were
responsible for managing the growing complexity of digital
marketing channels and tools, ensuring lead attribution, and
optimising campaigns. However, like SalesOps, MarketingOps
remained isolated from other functions. Each department continued
to work in silos, with minimal alignment around shared revenue
goals, which led to friction across the customer lifecycle.
The Evolution of Customer Success
In the mid-2000s, especially within the Software-as-a-Service (SaaS)
industry, the importance of customer retention came into sharper
focus. Businesses realised that acquiring new customers wasn’t
enough—retaining them was equally, if not more, critical. As a result,
Customer Success teams emerged, with a primary focus on helping
customers achieve their desired outcomes and ensuring long-term
satisfaction.
Customer Success Operations (CSOps) followed shortly after,
focusing on optimising processes that would drive customer

---

## Page 15

retention and expansion. But like SalesOps and MarketingOps before
it, CSOps remained disconnected from the sales and marketing
processes, exacerbating the fragmented customer experience.
The Emergence of RevOps
RevOps was born out of the recognition that aligning these disparate
functions—SalesOps, MarketingOps, and CSOps—was essential for
driving sustainable revenue growth. Around 15 years ago, forward-
thinking organisations began to implement RevOps as a unified
framework to address the long-standing challenges of siloed
departments. RevOps sought to streamline processes, improve data
sharing, and ensure all revenue-generating teams worked towards
shared goals.
This approach went beyond just combining teams; it fostered true
alignment through standardised processes, shared metrics, and the
integration of technology platforms. RevOps provided a holistic view
of the customer journey, allowing teams to collaborate more
effectively and deliver a seamless customer experience.
RevOps Today: A Mature Framework
As RevOps matured over the past decade and a half, it has adapted
to meet the evolving needs of businesses. Initially viewed as a
response to the growing complexity of revenue generation, RevOps
has now become a critical driver of efficiency and alignment in
modern organisations. It has enabled companies to scale more
effectively, optimise their operations, and provide better experiences
for their customers.
With the rise of digital transformation, artificial intelligence (AI), and
automation, RevOps has expanded its scope. Today, businesses use
RevOps not only to align sales, marketing, and customer success but
also to leverage data, automate workflows, and create predictive
insights that drive decision-making.
