WHAT THIS BOOK IS AND
HOW IT CAN HELP YOU
The average founder will go through 2.1 sales leaders between
Series A and Series B.[1]
One of the reasons for this high failure rate is that founders with
a product background are poor at hiring “go to market” leaders that
can successfully take ownership of one of the most strategic parts of
your business: generating revenue.
Technical CEOs don’t have the experience of scaling a go-to-
market team, and you’ll need to rely on what you are told by the
people you bring in to lead each function.
You can easily fall into common traps that harm your ability to
grow:
●       You might be advised that generating revenue is all about
selling, when you need to focus on helping your customers
to buy.
●       You might hire overly experienced GTM leaders too early,
when right now you need someone focused on execution.
●       You might hire the right individual too late, with an over
reliance on promoting inexperienced internal hires who are
figuring it out for the first time..
●       You might allow newly hired leaders to create their own
silos, and suddenly one company feels like three.
There is a transition underway in B2B selling that is more
transformational than at any time since the arrival of the internet.
Today, buyers have access to so much more information and are
so much better connected to their peers that it is no longer good
enough for a company to sell well.

## Page 9

According to Gartner, a management consulting company,
buyers spend only 17% of their buying process in front of vendors—
and assuming they are looking at three different suppliers. That
means only 5%-6% of their time is in front of your sales team.
Selling well is not enough.
For companies to be successful they need to help their
customers to buy.
Gartner describes this as buyer enablement,[2] and we’ll look at
how you can develop these skills in more detail throughout the book.
For many CEOs, delegating the revenue responsibility to your
marketing, sales, and customer success leaders too early results in
individual functions being good at their own tasks while the buyer
receives a fragmented experience as they get handed from one team
to the next.
Deals slip, the company misses targets, leadership turnover
ensures, and you, the CEO, are required to take the reins again to
reboot the go to market function.
This Revenue Operations Playbook is for you as a technical
founder and a product-centric CEO. It will give you a clear overview
of what is required to build a predictable, repeatable, and consistent
revenue engine.
If you are in the immediate aftermath of your Series A funding,
this book will help you to lead the go to market function as the
founder’s responsibility, before delegating aspects of the revenue
engine to your new leaders.
If you are at a later stage and the silos have already crept in, or
you are experiencing failures in your go to market function, then this
book will help you refocus and renew your revenue strategy.
While this playbook is applicable to many industries, my
background is Enterprise SaaS and many of the lessons are drawn
from that experience.
Wherever you are on your founder journey, this playbook will
ensure you can have an educated conversation with your leadership
team, reduce the risk of silos, missed targets and failed hires, and
allow you to focus on the strategic direction of your company.

Understanding A Company At Series A And
Series B
Let’s start by understanding what a typical Series A company looks
like.
Of course it will vary depending on your customer segment,
your geography and where the world is in an economic cycle, but
according to data from Carta (an equity management platform) and
ICONIQ (a venture firm) a company raising Series A will raise around
$6.4m at a pre-money valuation of $40m.
A company at this stage will have around $3m in annual
recurring revenue (ARR) being generated across a team of about 30
people in the company.
By Series B that company has grown from $3m ARR to around
$10m ARR, and their team has grown from those 30 team members
to around 100.
Note: Although the actual numbers will vary with economic
cycles, it is the relative difference between Series A and B that is
important to recognize.

Your Team Will Grow
Now, let’s look at how your team will change over this next phase of
your business.
Again calling on data from SaaStr[5] and ICONIQ, at Series A, a
company in its entirety is around 30 people. Of those 15 are in some
kind of go-to-market role, and perhaps eight will be in a sales role.
But by Series B, in just four quarters if we are following the
T2D3 track, the company will have around 100 people in it, with 70
of those being in the go-to-market team, and 40 being in a sales
role.
Let’s look at these two GTM teams in more detail to understand
the changes required to go from 15 to 70 in such a short period of
time.

The GTM Org at $3m ARR
At $3m ARR you are still moving from founder-led sales to your first
formal sales leader—typically a sales director. That sales leader will
be in a player/manager role and will focus on selling alongside their
team, maybe even carrying their own quota.
At this stage it is you, the founder, who is the only person who
has full responsibility across all stages of the buyer experience from
marketing, to sales and on to customer success.
There may be one marketing or sales operations role to support
the GTM function, but a formal revenue operations role has not been
hired yet.
The GTM Org at $10m ARR
By the time you reach $10m ARR you will have built out a more
senior leadership team. This will most commonly be built from new
hires with experience of scaling larger teams rather than internal
promotions.
Your leaders are now more focused on process than execution,
but you still won’t have a single executive below yourself as the CEO

Problems In This Phase
With Series A funding in the bank, you can easily get tripped up by
accelerating when you don’t yet have the right foundations to build
on.
As we go through this playbook I will guide you through how to
mitigate these challenges, so don’t panic yet. But I do want to
highlight these common traps.
No one person with full GTM experience
Your marketing, sales, and customer success leaders are
practitioners for their own function, and your company strives
forward with no one person looking at the end to end buyer journey.
At this stage you, the CEO, are the only person who has full
responsibility across marketing, sales, and customer success.
As we’ll see through the book you have a role to keep these
three functions aligned: for you, for your team, and importantly, for
your customers.
Lack of quality pipeline
As you expand your company into new markets, new customer
segments, and new products, you’ll scale up your sales teams.
One of the biggest challenges you’ll face is lack of pipeline for
your new sellers to sell into.
An internally focused rev ops team won’t solve your pipeline
challenge. You need them to be externally focused on your buyers’
needs—their pains and desired gains—and to continually define a
value proposition that drives pipeline growth.

WHAT IS REVENUE
OPERATIONS?
We aren’t going to spend much time defining the topic because we’ll
learn more about it as we go through each chapter.
But it is likely you will have purchased this book with a
preconceived idea of what RevOps is, and I want to explode that
before we start.
RevOps is often defined as an internally focused tactical role.
Your RevOps specialist will work on your CRM-updating fields,
generating that forecast report you need for the board meeting, or
designing your sales reps’ commission plan.
Many RevOps roles are filled with ex SalesOps or MarketingOps
candidates who have grown up with this internal focus.
Senior GTM leaders reinforce it further by treating RevOps
individuals as their doers, locked away in a bunker somewhere
generating reports and process documentation.
It is easy to fall into the trap that RevOps is a support function,
not strategic, and something you can delegate on day one.
You’d be wrong.

Revenue Operations Is Strategic
In the book Revenue Operations by Stephen Diorio and Chris
Hummel[6] that was published in 2022, the authors interviewed a
number of senior executives across a range of industries.
They noted that “while most agree that revenue operations is a
good thing…more than 90% of the senior executives we
spoke with were not clear on what exactly a Revenue
Operations model meant or how exactly it will pay off.”
The authors go on to say, “Describing Revenue Operations is
similar to describing great art, writing, or music. Most people agree
it is a good thing and everybody seems to know it when they see it,
but few people can describe it.”
To put it another way, senior executives in companies much
bigger and further along than yours have no idea how to define
revenue operations.
This is your opportunity—your opportunity to define what
revenue operations will mean in the context of your business.
The model I am about to lay out will help you challenge the
leaders you hire as they bring in their internally focused view that in
many cases will miss the strategic value of a revenue operations
model.

## Page 31

Revenue Operations Helps Customers To Buy
Revenue operations is the strategy of aligning marketing, sales, and
customer success into a single revenue engine.
In the past, companies would pass their buyers from team to
team as they filtered down their sales funnel.
Marketing would market to prospects.
Sales would sell to opportunities.
Customer success would onboard customers and support them.
Each team had a different set of content, a different language, a
different tone of voice.
At each handover the customer felt they were dealing with an
entirely new company and had to explain themselves all over again.
But today the buyer is in charge.
Buyers no longer rely on vendors to be their only source of
information.
They speak to their peers.
They engage in private online communities.
They discuss with their trusted partners.
According to Gartner, buyers will travel 57% of their way along
the buying process before engaging with a vendor.[7]
When buyers do want information from a vendor, they want
access to details from every step of your “sales” process at once.
They want pricing before they do a demo.
They want to see the onboarding process before they see a
contract.
They want to speak with other customers before they attend an
event.

Defining Jobs To Be Done
When you buy a product or service, you’re not buying that thing,
you are hiring it to perform some kind of job for you.
When you buy an umbrella, you don’t want an umbrella but you
hire it to stop the rain falling on you.
When you buy a CRM system you don’t want a CRM system, but
you hire it so that you have some predictability and repeatability
around your sales process and forecasting.
This concept of looking at your customer and considering the
jobs they need to do to be successful in their role and in their
personal lives is at the heart of JTBD.
We don’t create new products for the sake of it, we create them
to help our customers perform their jobs.
Three types of jobs to be done
So what is a job?

---

## Page 45

The main job
Every product or service has a main functional job that you hire it
for.
The umbrella needs to keep the rain off you. If it doesn’t, then
it fails in that main job.
At the heart of a CRM system is the tracking and reporting of
your customer’s and seller’s activities. This allows you to forecast
accurately and ensure you deliver a high quality service to your
customers.
This is the main job. For a CRM system to be “hired” it needs to
be able to deliver on this core requirement.
The emotional job
Beyond the main job, we hire products or services to make us feel
differently about ourselves.

---

## Page 46

We might book a holiday to a specific destination to make us
feel great beyond the main job of time away from work.
As the CEO you need to make decisions about the future of the
business, and to do that you need confidence in what will happen in
the future.
Will those deals close this month? Are your team building
accurate reports in it? Are you confident in your numbers when you
step into the board meeting? Does the CRM give you the confidence
to spend your time on other strategic areas of the business?
These are the emotional jobs that the CRM needs to perform for
you.
The social job
The third type of job we ask products or services to perform is
defined by what others think about us when we use the product or
service.
When we buy a certain car or brand of phone, it is as much for
what other people think of us as it is for our own needs.
When you pick a CRM what do your investors think, what do
your partners think, what do your employees think?
The four forces of change
Next up is understanding the forces that define a buyer’s process of
changing from one solution to another, or from the status quo to a
new way.

---

## Page 47

When you hire a new product or service you are affected by four
forces:
Push - the initiation of anything is being dissatisfied in some
way with the way things are today. In the case of a CRM, you might
find it difficult to keep track of opportunities in spreadsheets.
There has to be some kind of push, because if you don’t have
an issue with the status quo, then the other forces will prevail.
Pull - the second force starts to pull you toward the new way.
You started to look at possible solutions, you took the test drive, you
signed up for the free trial, and now you’re starting to think about
the new world and it feels good.
With your CRM you might attend an event or speak to some
portfolio peers about the system they are using—the future seems
bright!
But, while the push and pull draw you toward the new world,
there are two very powerful forces that counteract and lure you back
to the status quo.

---

## Page 48

Habit - humans are creatures of habit. For example, I always
drive the same way to work. After a break I’ll head back to the same
seat in a conference.
Habit is a strong force. Although your spreadsheet forecast is
not ideal, you are a small team still and the process just about
works.
Maybe you can survive with it for a few more quarters..
Anxiety – When we start to think about the new way our stress
levels start to increase. This might be cost related—buyer’s remorse
in advance—“Should I spend this money?”, but often it relates to
competing priorities and taking a team off one project for another.
For a CRM system there is a lot of change required: new
processes, new integrations, change enablement for the sellers, and
a sizable invoice that will arrive before we even start the project.
In business we think the customer is only focused on our
features and pricing, but they are more likely focused on the need
for their team to learn something new, to implement and integrate
the tool, and to prevent the risk of it not working. Do we need to do
this right now?
The four forces of push, pull, habit, and anxiety are in constant
tension in your buyers as they explore problems their company faces
and the opportunities in front of them.

Uncovering the dark funnel
The dark funnel is a term coined by the technology company

being able to track them.
Let’s make it easier to understand by thinking about the
opposite—the light funnel, if you will.

interactions they had with your brand on their journey to becoming a
customer.
1. They came to our website
2. They registered for a webinar
3. They attended an event
4. They downloaded a whitepaper
5. They took a call from an sales development rep (SDR)
6. They spoke to our account executive (AE)
7. They signed a contract

You’ll speak to your friends, read auto magazines, read

do arrive there, you’ll likely know the model, the engine, the color,
and the price you want to pay.
You know the dealer is there to serve their own interests, not
yours, and so you’ll get yourself educated before you walk in the
door.
Why should we think it is any different in business?
It’s not.

CHANNELS TO HELP YOUR
CUSTOMER TO BUY
When I started selling in 1999, much of technology was sold through
partners.
The CDs needed installing, the servers required connecting, the
cables needed plugging in, and for many smaller companies this
meant working with a local IT service provider.
Companies like Microsoft, Cisco, and Symantec developed global
partner programs, with multiple tiers, education, incentives, and
marketing support to assist this army of hundreds of thousands of
service providers in promoting, selling, implementing, and
supporting their customers.
Two main models existed:
Distribution and Resellers - (often called VADs and VARs) -
these partners owned the billing and support relationship with the
customer buying at a discount off list price from the vendor.
Affiliates - (or called associates or referral partners) - these
partners passed qualified leads into the sales team at the vendor in
return for a commission (typically 10%-20%) of the first year sales
price.
Each of these models gave vendors different benefits:
Access to the market - a global partner program could give a
tech company access to customers and opportunities they could
never get to directly with their own sales teams: new geographies or
into companies that had locked down purchasing to an approved
vendor list.
Reduced cost of sale - while they needed to pay the partner a
margin on top of the partner manager in their team, the economics

---

## Page 116

of this were better compared to maintaining a large direct sales
team for these deals.
Reduced cost of serve - the partner was onsite and had the
support relationship with the customer, meaning fewer support calls
to the vendor themselves. Even in an affiliate model where the
partner was not paid to provide support, they often had the answer
at hand avoiding a call to the vendor.
Cloud went direct
The arrival of cloud solutions in the mid-2000s changed this
approach for many newer vendors.
“If we don’t need to send anything physical to our customer, do
we need a partner to manage the customer relationship and eat into
our margins?”
Companies like Salesforce came to market without any
meaningful reseller or referral programs.
(Salesforce does provide referral margins to partners under very
strict circumstances, but it is a running joke in the partner
community that it’s almost impossible to qualify for the payment.)
Most SaaS vendors copied this model. In researching this book
I’ve looked at 50 SaaS businesses, and just a handful have any form
of partner channel visible on their website.
Where partners are listed these are typically technology and
integration partnerships rather than sales channels.
But SaaS is coming back to partners
In G2’s 2022 Software Buyer Behavior Report[20] they note the
percentage of cloud software being bought directly from vendors is
starting to decrease again, as customers try to consolidate the
spend, the management, and the security of sometimes hundreds of
different platforms.

If you don’t have those channels open, then you are missing out

Blending Product Led Growth With Enterprise
Sales
As buyers become more self-sufficient in their research and their
onboarding, even vendors focused on the largest enterprise deals
are looking at how to blend the benefits of a seller-free experience
with their enterprise sales teams.
Some of the most successful companies have followed a
product-led growth (PLG) strategy.
●       I bought and set up my company’s Gmail and Google Drive
with no seller.
●       I bought and set up my company’s accounting with Xero
with no seller.
●       I bought and built my company’s website with Squarespace
with no seller.
This doesn’t work for every category. Big enterprise platforms
like ERP, Payroll, and HR are top-down purchases requiring formal
projects with complex integrations and data migrations.
But for the majority of software categories, buyers expect to be
able to test, learn, and buy without having to interact with the
vendor.
As a company’s usage gets more complex, or their needs take
them toward higher value products, then it does make sense to
introduce sales support to guide them through the enterprise buying
process.
Case study: Amplitude

---

## Page 125

In a recent SaaStr presentation, Mark Velthuis, VP at Amplitude,
shared his strategy which he calls Product Led Sales.[21]
There are four steps:
Great product - you have to have a fantastic product that is
easy to use and that people will talk about.
If there is any friction in the trial, purchase, or onboarding
process then you can’t use your product to drive growth.
Freemium model - you have to provide your users with a free
way of onboarding and getting value from your product.
This isn’t as effective if you provide a time-bound free trial. You
need to give lifetime access and value. Great examples of this are
Canva, Gmail, or Loom.
Product Signals - you then want to determine what usage of
your freemium platform indicates the right time to contact the
customer with a real seller.
Mark offers two examples.
Firstly, when the customer is using 80%-90% of the freemium
unit of measurement, maybe users, or storage or events.
Secondly, when the customer starts adding in users from
different functions or business units.
Both of these are triggers to reach out and see whether now is
the right time to benefit from some of the paid features.
Mark calls them PQL’s: product-qualified leads. As a seller you
would be all over a PQL compared to an MQL!
Train your sellers - this outreach is different to your normal
cold outreach.
These are now happy customers, who are familiar with your
product.
It is a warmer and gentler approach focused on education and
expansion, more akin to a CSM than an SDR.
Your sellers need to have a much higher technical knowledge of
your products as they will be interacting with educated customers.

from his sales teams.

Four main SaaS agreements
The agreements that your company uses will be dependent on the

Non-Disclosure Agreement - this is often signed during a

INCREASE YOUR
RENEWALS AND DRIVE
DOWN CHURN
SaaS businesses have historically been highly valued by the public
markets because their revenues recur.
At scale, allowing for a percentage of churn, the majority of
next year’s revenues are already contractually committed before the
year even starts, allowing your sales teams to focus on adding more
new customers into the pot.
As customers become ever more focused on their own spend,
and the number of competitive products compete for your
customer’s attention, you’ll need a clear focus on renewing your
customers and minimizing the churn that eats away at your
committed annual recurring revenue.
I always visualize a bath when I think of churn.
The taps are running full speed as your sales teams work their
socks off to close new business.
But if the plug is out and water is pouring out at the same
speed, then the bath isn’t filling up and your ARR isn’t growing, or at
best isn’t growing as fast as it could be.
Learn renewal skills now to benefit later
Although this chapter is focused on the importance of renewals and
churn reduction, it is important to remember that at Series A
through to Series B, 70% of your revenue comes from new 
customers.

---

## Page 194

That is to say that as you are scaling up, it is your ability to win
new customers and expand your addressable market that is critical.
However, the skills that your team learns now about how to
retain and renew customers will become critical as you move past
Series B and $10m ARR. Better to learn them now than have to
recover churn issues when they are more painful lessons.
You will find two main metrics in relation to churn and renewals:
Net Revenue Retention (NRR): This looks at your overall
growth (or reduction) across a set of accounts year on year. Say a
set of accounts spent $100 with you last year, NRR will show that
that set of customers might spend $130 with you this year, a growth
of 30%. You might have lost a handful of customers due to churn,
but because your account managers added on some additional users
and products the overall base increased their spend.
NRR is a common measure of churn because it shows the power
of a SaaS business. For any set of customers, their spend will
continue to increase over time regardless of any new customers
being won.
The downside of NRR is that it can mask the true loss of actual
customers which is covered by:
Gross Revenue Retention (GRR): This looks at the same set
of accounts and asks what they spend this year compared to last
year, but disregarding any additional expansion.
It clearly shows that out of the $100 this set of customers spent
last year, this year they spent $93. This means we have lost $7
because customers downsized, canceled, or went out of business.
Another version of gross revenue retention is gross logo
retention where we look at the number of customers rather than
their spend. Out of 100 customers last year we still have 97 of them
today.

Renewal Happens 365 Days A Year
An easy trap for SaaS businesses to fall into is to treat customer
contracts as an activity planner.
With an annual contract, let’s set a trigger 60 days out to
discuss renewal.
With a two-year contract we can schedule that in 22 months’
time.
And for the three-year contracts 34 months’ time.

carpet until there is a real contractual risk that the customer might
leave us.
But the reality is if you wait until then to uncover if the
customer is finding value in your product it is too late.
Customers despise vendors who only contact them at contract
renewal time:
“Hi, we’ve not met but I am your new account manager.
You’re coming up to your renewal and I wanted to let you know
that due to the ‘innovation’ we have released in the last year the
pricing has gone up 10%. And good news, I can do you a deal on
the new ACME product we just launched…”
It is not the most appealing experience for your customer.

contract—delivering incremental value that means renewal is the
default not a negotiation.

When I started in SaaS multi-year contracts were the default.

multi-year maintenance contracts and so for most companies it felt
natural to commit to a two-, three-, or even five-year contract.
Today though customers are wary of being locked in as they
have so many choices and relatively low cost of switching.

## Page 196

Use A Goal Setting Framework To Focus The
Team On What Matters Most
Having defined a clear three year vision, the next step is to help
your team understand how to get there.
If the vision is the top of the mountain, then we now need to
plot the path in bite sized quarterly chunks.
You may well already have a goal-setting framework in the
company and even if you do, let’s look at some best practice.
The OKR framework stands for objectives and key results.
It was popularized by Andy Grove, the legendary CEO of Intel,
in the 1990s, and then hit the mainstream at Google before being
picked up by many tech companies along with non-profits, including
the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation and Bono’s ONE campaign.[31]
The framework is built into three main elements:

---

## Page 212

●       Objective: What is the goal we are trying to achieve?
●       Key Result: How will we know we achieved the goal?
●       Initiative: What will we do to achieve the key results?
If we take the example of a trip to New York:
●       Objective: We will enjoy an amazing week in New York
●       Key Results: Taxi arrived on time, plane took off, plane
landed, we checked into the hotel
●       Initiatives: search for flights, call taxi companies, pack
suitcase
What makes a good objective?
An objective should be clearly understood by anyone who reads it
regardless of the team they are in.
In our example anyone could repeat the goal to have an
amazing week in New York and know what it means.
Objectives, like our vivid vision, should be aspirational but
achievable. They should be meaningful to the business and align the
teams to something they believe can be achieved.
An amazing customer experience.
The best onboarding experience possible.
An overflowing pipeline.
What makes a good key result?
While the objective is qualitative, the key results are very
quantitative. They either happened or they did not.
A key result usually has some type of number attached to it, or
it is binary: on or off.
In our example the taxi did arrive on time or it did not.
The plane did take off or it did not.
The plane did land or it did not.
In a business context examples might be:
Pipeline of new business opportunities is > $1m
Average sales cycle < 3 months

---

## Page 213

Customer net promoter score is > 30
When considering key results ensure that:
a)      Completing all the key results ensures completion of the
objective
b)      The key results are aspirational and yet achievable
c)       There is a named lead for each key result
How is an initiative different from a key result?
An initiative is something that you will do, whereas a key result is
something that has happened (or will have happened).
The initiatives are lists of things that your team will do in order
to achieve the key result.
In our example the initiatives include searching for a flight,
booking a taxi, packing your bags.
It is important to call out this difference, because often a lazy
OKR setting starts to include initiatives as key results.
When this happens, the key results become just a list of things
you are planning to do (recruit more partners, request more
referrals) and don’t necessarily ensure the delivery of the objective
or give a concrete yes/no to whether the key results was achieved.

Beware of cascading objectives
In any goal setting framework the temptation is to cascade goals
from a corporate level to a team level to an individual level.
Cascading means that at any level your objectives are a subset
of the objectives at the level above.
The idea is that this ensures every individual can connect their
work up through the hierarchy to the company strategy.
The reality is that this introduces friction and delays to the goal
setting process.
The company releases the corporate level goals on schedule,
but then it is up to functional leaders, and then their management
team to create their own goals at a time when they are busy kicking
off the year with their teams.
These goals either never arrive or are poorly thought through.
The managers have taken the wind out of the goal setting
process and the individual goal setting becomes a tick box exercise
in an HR system.
At worst the individual goals are not set until the end of the
quarter and become a “So what did you do last quarter?” reverse-
engineering process.
Two ways to avoid this:
Roll out in phases from top to bottom.
To avoid goal setting being a management tool that is required
of individual contributors but is not subscribed to by senior leaders
and management, start with you and your senior leadership team
over one or two quarters.
Get into the rhythm of it yourself, organize the team workshops
each quarter. Focus on the discussion not the systems.
Publish your OKRs and your progress toward them transparently
and on every team call.
After a few quarters introduce team level OKRs with your senior
leaders setting their own OKRs for their own teams.
They may include members of their own teams to support the
initiatives but the creation and management of the OKRs is the team

---

## Page 218

leader’s responsibility.
After another few quarters, once OKRs has become embedded
behavior for your management team, then and only then can you
start to roll the framework out at an individual level.
By now everyone in the team can see how OKRs are the
foundation of how the company decides what is important and how
we know when we have achieved it.
They know that this is a framework that helps us all achieve
something remarkable and not a micro-management tool.
Allow “orphan” OKRs
When you set your OKR schedule, allow individuals to create
their own OKRs without waiting for their manager or team leaders to
create theirs.
Your corporate OKRs will be published on schedule and that is
enough for an individual to set their own OKRs: some tied to the
overall corporate goals, and some personal relating to their own
career trajectory.
OKRs are a journey
Whether you use OKRs or any other goal-setting framework, it needs
to fit into your own company’s culture and way of working.
There will be times when you need to revise or revisit your own

But this doesn’t mean goal setting doesn’t work.
Having a defined goal for this quarter, for this year, and indeed

all your new starters on the work ahead.
To quote the US baseball player Yogi Berra: “If you don’t know

Choosing between seat-based and usage-based
pricing
Choosing the wrong pricing model can kill your SaaS business, or at
least hamper your growth.
One of the big decisions to make is between a seat-based model
(how many human beings are using the platform) or a usage-based
model (how much do your customers use your product).
Seat-based model
Also called a user-based model.
Examples of this are Salesforce, Workday, Google Workspace—
you pay for the number of people that you want to have an account.
Each of these products is a company-wide platform—every
employee gets Gmail and Google Calendar, every salesperson gets
CRM.
There is little decision for the customer to make when they hire
(or layoff) team members. We need more (or fewer) licenses.
Usage-based model
Also called a consumption- or transaction-based model.

---

## Page 290

Examples are the main cloud platforms: Azure, AWS, and GCP,
data platforms like Snowflake and Databricks, communications
platforms like Twilio, or financial platforms like Stripe.
It doesn’t matter how many people use the platform (probably a
smaller number of developers and data analysts). Instead, the
platform is charged on how many transactions are put through:
●       Data volumes stored
●       SMSs sent
●       Dollars processed
The companies listed above are quite clear cut in which model is
best. And indeed multi-product companies like Google have different
models for different products (Google Cloud Platform and Adwords—
usage based; Google Workspace—seat based)
But what happens when it is not so clear which model to
choose? Imagine a product that does not have to be used by every
employee.
To keep things simple, let’s assume that an average seat
consumes 100 transactions per annum, and a seat is charged at 100
times the transaction cost., i.e., there is no commercial difference
between the two models if users follow the average usage.

you can charge more today and typically lock in the revenue up

Humans Are Terrible At Forecasting Even

In David Epstein’s book Range,[43] Epstein talks about humans’ ability
to forecast.
He discusses an experiment during the Cold War to assess the
predictions of 284 highly educated experts, most with doctorates.
The experts were asked to predict specific future events,
covering a wide range of topics including international politics and
economics.
The project ran over 20 years and collated 82,361 predictions.
“The average expert was a terrible forecaster. They were bad at
short-term forecasting, bad at long-term forecasting, and bad at
forecasting in every domain.”
“When experts declared that some future event was impossible
or nearly impossible, it nonetheless occurred 15% of the time. When
they declared a sure thing, it failed to transpire more than a quarter
of the time.”
Even when they aren’t skewed by their own personal career
ambitions and needs, humans are just not very good at predicting
what will happen in the future.

managers to guide us in what is one of the most important sources
